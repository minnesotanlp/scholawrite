ScholaWrite

Decoding How Scientists Write — from First Keystroke to Final Manuscript

Khanh Chi Le, Linghe Wang, Minhwa Lee, Ross Volkov, Luan Tuyen Chau, Dongyeop Kang

Minnesota NLP — University of Minnesota

Paper Dataset Code

Abstract

Writing is a cognitively demanding activity that requires constant decision-making, heavy reliance on working memory, and frequent shifts between tasks of different goals. To build writing assistants that truly align with writers' cognition, we must capture and decode the complete thought process behind how writers transform ideas into final texts. We contribute: (1) a Chrome extension that unobtrusively records keystrokes on Overleaf, enabling realistic, in-situ writing data collection; (2) a novel corpus of full scholarly manuscripts enriched with fine-grained annotations of cognitive writing intentions; and (3) analyses revealing the micro-dynamics of scholarly writing, highlighting gaps between human writing processes and the current capabilities of large language models. ScholaWrite underscores the value of capturing end-to-end writing data to develop future writing assistants that support, not replace, the cognitive work of scientists.

Key Contributions

Three advances toward understanding the cognitive dynamics of scholarly writing.

1

Unobtrusive Data Collection

A Chrome extension that silently records every keystroke on Overleaf—insertions, deletions, copy-paste, and reverts—capturing realistic writing behavior without disrupting the author's flow.

2

Intention-Annotated Corpus

Five complete research manuscripts enriched with expert annotations mapping each text change to one of 15 cognitive writing intentions, grounded in established writing process theory.

3

Human vs. LLM Analysis

In-depth analyses of writing micro-dynamics revealing where current LLMs fall short of mimicking the complex, non-linear cognitive processes that human researchers employ.

Writing Intention Taxonomy

Every text change is annotated with one of 15 cognitive intentions, spanning the full spectrum of scholarly writing—from initial brainstorming to final polish.
