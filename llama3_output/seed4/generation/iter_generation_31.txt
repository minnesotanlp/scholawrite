bihari2023fading} on a ten-word paragraph, we choose sentences of lengths 7, 9, and 10, which represent a minimal, medium, and maximal exposure to the contextual information of other sentences in the paragraph, a scenario akin to the constrained ``fill-in-the-gap'' task in \textsc{Unified Book}\footnote{The 1-word completion task in \textsc{Unified Book} has a longer story context like our setup, but without the unconstrained part of the paragraph.}. 

\textbf{Findings}
Our findings (see Fig~\ref{fig:dist_diff_five_domains}) show that the three domains of text - ``yelp,'' ``cmv,'' and ``xsum'' - exhibit the highest human perception scores. Notably, within these three domains, ``yelp'' stands out with a significant gap between its human perception score and the average human perception score of the other two domains. This suggests that domain-specific distinctiveness can arise from human-authored texts while machine-generated texts seem relatively general. 

When examining the  and  top 5\% of sentence pairs that exhibit the highest human perception scores versus those of machine-generated perception scores, we observe a distinct pattern (see Fig~\ref{fig:corr_per_domain}). We found that almost all of the pairs of sentences with the highest human perception scores have a correlation of 0.7 or higher, whereas machine-generated sentences have a correlation of -0.7 or lower. This suggests that human-authored texts are strongly correlated while machine-generated texts are not.
