\subsection{Suggestions for each data type}
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep,topsep=5pt,leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Free-Form Texts}: Mitigate the ``curse of generality'' in language models by incorporating domain-specific information during text generation, using few-shot prompting with relevant examples. Enhance discourse structure modeling or adjust decoding strategies to improve the human-like quality of machine-generated texts, such as re-ranking candidates based on high-level metrics. Moreover, in the evaluation of machine-generated texts, it could be beneficial to incorporate high-level features such as the \textit{``formality score,''} \textit{``extent of label flipping,''} and \textit{``discourse motif distribution,''} in conjunction with conventional metrics like ROUGE, BLEU, or METEOR. This approach enhances the comprehensiveness of the evaluation, enabling a more nuanced assessment of the resemblance between human-written and machine-generated texts within a given domain.
\item \textbf{Task Labels}: Majority voting, commonly used for generating task labels, tends to overlook minority views and disagreements among annotators, particularly in highly subjective tasks. Rather than evaluating LLM task labels solely on cost, speed, and accuracy of annotation, we propose alternative metrics like \textit{label distributional difference} or an analysis of the \textit{variation in individual text annotations} to highlight discrepancies between LLM and human-generated labels. It's crucial to validate LLMs with human datasets to prevent data biases and falsely inflated performance metrics, underscoring the importance of establishing clear guidelines for various tasks. 
\item \textbf{Instructions}: We found that synthetic datasets contain various types of errors and that artificially adding errors to fine-tuning data resulted in worse performance. We recommend that the reader mitigate these issues by closely \textit{inspecting data for errors}, performing automatic preprocessing, and generating diverse data. Researchers may want to consider creating datasets following observed patterns or tendencies to better understand how different types of errors affect instruction-tuning data.
