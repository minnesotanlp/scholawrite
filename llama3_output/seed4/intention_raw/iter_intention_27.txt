\subsubsection{First Order Effects}\label{sec:pref-first-results}
\paragraph{Hypothesis} We explore how LLM preferences adhere more closely to rudimentary heuristics than human preferences do. This suggests that LLM preferences may be more superficial than human preferences are.

\paragraph{Experiment Setup} In our analysis of sentence pairs from the \textsc{p2c} dataset, we focus on identifying the most important stylistic elements that are also present in the \textsc{hummingbird} dataset \cite{hayati2021does} and have the highest human perception scores. For sentence pairs of positive sentiment, we extract lexicons associated with high perception scores for emotions like \textit{joy}, \textit{politeness}, and \textit{positive sentiment}. Conversely, for the pairs of negative sentiments, we select lexicons related to \textit{anger}, \textit{disgust}, \textit{fear}, \textit{sadness}, \textit{offensiveness}, \textit{rudeness}, and \textit{negative sentiment}.

Once we identify the most important lexicon with the highest perception score for each sentence, we compare their human perception scores to determine which sentence is more showing a higher degree of gold-label sentiment. For example, if a sentence of positive sentiment has a higher score of ``joy'' than its counterpart in the pair, that sentence is deemed the ``winner.'' We apply this method across various styles and emotions within the same sentiment category. The sentence that wins in the majority of these style categories is then considered the preferred sentence, a decision we term as ``lexicon-based preference.''
