\subsection{Summary of First and Second Order Results}
As outlined in the results section, LLMs tend to under-represent minority opinions. ChatGPT especially grossly under-represents minority annotations while over-representing majority annotations. This risks marginalizing minority perspectives, potentially reinforcing societal disparities \cite{prabhakaran2021releasing}. For subjective tasks, we find that the ChatGPT model exhibits less variation in its responses to subjective tasks than humans, which could indicate oversimplification of these tasks or high predictive confidence. Additionally, we noted that datasets with more labels, such as Sentiment and Schem5Labels, demonstrate more variation in human judgment, particularly in subjective tasks like controversial content. Conversely, simpler datasets with fewer labels, like SBIC and GHC, display lesser human variation and greater alignment with the model, indicating more consistent annotations in less complex content. This pattern highlights that the model's performance and concordance with human annotations significantly depend on the task's complexity and subjective elements. On analyzing text instances with significant human annotation variation but no model variation (especially in cases where the human minority label is not the same as the model majority label), we find this an effective heuristic to unearth highly biased sentences. We note the presence of age, gender, political, and religion-based bias, which are indications of the under-representation of minorities or complexities in training data.
