\section{Free Text}
We examine how high-level attributes, particularly discourse structures and textual styles, differ between machine- and human-written texts. Our experiments suggest that machines engage with context and social interactions more superficially than humans do. We show how these differences can be related to classifier performance when machine-generated texts are used in the training pipeline.

\paragraph{Background} Collecting human-authored texts that fit particular desired attributes, domains, or topics is highly resource-intensive. Recent research investigates using these machine-authored texts to augment or replace human-authored texts, e.g., so that the texts can be used to train sequence classifiers \cite{moller2023prompt, guo2023-how-close}. This trend is partly motivated by findings that machine-generated text is difficult to distinguish from human text \cite{clark-etal-2021-thats, li2023deepfake}. Previous work differentiates between human- and machine-authored text primarily through data-driven methods with trained text classifiers. Instead, we aim to qualitatively probe for artifacts in machine-generated text by examining linguistic features, specifically \textit{discourse structures} and \textit{textual style}.

\paragraph{Data}
We use datasets the \textsc{HC3} \cite{guo2023close}, \textsc{Scarecrow} \cite{dou-etal-2022-gpt}, \textsc{Deepfake} \cite{li2023deepfake}, and \textsc{Workers} \cite{moller2023prompt}. We choose these datasets because of their paired human-machine format, which enables us to compare the difference between human-written and LLM-generated texts. 

\paragraph{First Order Experiments}
\noindent \textit{High-level attributes} We first explore how LLM-generated and human-written texts differ concerning high-level attributes, especially those concerning textual style and humor, hypothesizing that LLMs may not mirror humans very well in social and emotional contexts. We use the following high-level attributes and classifiers: formality \cite{rao2018dear}, emotion \cite{hartmann2022emotionenglish}, and irony \cite{barbieri2020tweeteval}, which are all primarily trained on social data (i.e., text from social media platforms or dialogues). We also employ a token-level classifier trained on the VUA Metaphor Corpus and the TOEFL corpus \cite{li2023framebert}. Our experiment is conducted on a series of paired human-machine texts from the \textsc{Workers} dataset, chosen because its social media domain best aligns with the classifiers' training domains and is well-suited to answer our research question regarding machine behavior in social contexts. In this dataset, GPT-4 is prompted with a target label (e.g. positive), domain (e.g. Twitter), and exemplary human text with the given domain and label. Following the human's example, the prompt asks GPT-4 to generate another text with the target domain and label. Our results indicate that the GPT-4 text is generally more formal than its human counterpart for these social contexts and employs more metaphoric tokens. We emphasize that the GPT-4 texts are generated in response to prompts that include the paired human text, meaning that the GPT-4 does not properly mimic all aspects of the human text's style. 

When we separate the results by the texts' target label, we note that humans' and GPT-4's style application varies based on domain (Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:high_level_features_radar}). In particular, GPT-4 applies \textit{joy} much more than humans for positive texts; humans apply \textit{anger} more than GPT-4 in texts that have the \textit{power social dynamic} label. We argue that this is indicative of GPT-4's shallow understanding of how contexts influence human emotion and socialization.
