\textbf{Impact of Sampling Strategy} :
Ego graph sampling \cite{spreen1994sampling} involves choosing a central node and its neighbors, creating a subgraph reflective of their interconnections. The chosen ego biases this method; for example, an unusually connected ego affects the subgraph's representativeness. Forest Fire sampling \cite{leskovec2006sampling}, in contrast, uses a spread-like mechanism starting from a random node to select network portions. Its randomness means subgraphs vary across instances, influenced by parameters like burning probabilities, and can be seen as a more exploratory method for the network's structure. Both methods have limitations and can skew perceptions of the network's overall structure, especially in large, diverse networks.

Forest Fire sampling is generally more likely to produce a sample representative of the entire network, especially for networks with a single large connected component (Pubmed). It has the potential to explore various regions of the network, not just the local neighborhood, which can be particularly important in large and complex networks.

Ego graph sampling, on the other hand, is better suited for studying the immediate local structure around a given node and can be more representative of the network if the goal is to understand the local community structure (CORA, Citeseer) rather than the global properties of the network.
