\subsection{From Local Discourse Motifs to Global Structural Patterns}\label{subsec:local_to_global}
We found the observation (\S\ref{subsubsec:key_motifs}) that human-written texts for more \textit{formal} and \textit{discussional} domains, e.g., news summarization (\textsc{XSum}) and ChangeMyView (\textsc{cmv}), seem to contain more ``Joint'' discourse relations where the corresponding child subgraphs are of equal importance (i.e., both are ``nuclei''). While this finding seems to corroborate our main assumption that the linguistic structure of discourse can change depending on factors such as formality, spontaneity, and depth of reasoning in the texts; it is based on the statistics of local discourse motifs albeit computed over the whole graphs. In this section, we aim to extend our analysis to include more \textit{global structures} by (1) computing the correlation between the number of hyperedge relations (denoted as ``hyp.'' in Fig. \ref{fig:hypergraph_example}) present in graphs and the formality score of the corresponding document (\S\ref{subsubsec:formality_scorer}); (2) investigating the overall shape variations of document graphs across different domains (\S\ref{subsubsec:branch_vs_chain}); and (3) comparing the variability in discourse structures of upper and lower parts of discourse graphs for original and paraphrased documents (\S\ref{subsubsec:upper_vs_lower}).

\subsubsection{Formality Scores and Hyperedges}\label{subsubsec:formality_scorer}

\begin{table}[!t]
    \centering
    \resizebox{0.75\columnwidth}{!}{
    \begin{tabular}{c|c}
        \toprule
        Machine-Generated & Human-Written\\
        \midrule
        $0.08^*$ & $0.39^*$ \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}}
    \caption{Pearson's $R$ between formality scores and frequency of hyperedges.}
    \label{tab:corr_formality_hyperedge}
\end{table}

The formality score is computed for every text generated by humans and machines. Also, for every corresponding document graph, we count the (normalized) frequency of hyperedges for all motifs present in the graph. This is because the hyperedge relations represent higher-level abstractions of text and are thus linked to the discourse hierarchy. 
From there, we compute the Pearson correlation coefficient between the scores and frequencies.

As shown in Table \ref{tab:corr_formality_hyperedge}, the human-written texts over all domains exhibit weak to moderate correlation with the formality style while machine-generated ones seem to show no correlation. This hints that when humans write more structured texts in terms of discourse, the contents are often more formal.

Another approach is to differentiate texts by domain and assume that the formality of texts is consistent within the domain. We can then look at the varying levels of graph structures per domain. As illustrated in the Introduction (\S\ref{sec:introduction}), we assume that the linguistic structure of discourse can change depending on factors such as formality, spontaneity, and depth of reasoning in the texts. 

One possible dimension of characterizing this difference in structures is by checking whether the structures are evenly branched or follow a more sequential pattern as illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:branch_vs_chain}.
