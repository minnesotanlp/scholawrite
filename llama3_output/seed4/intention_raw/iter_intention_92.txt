% overall themes 
Through our investigation into the five distinct LLM-generated data types, we notice thematic areas in which LLM-generated data falls short of its human counterpart:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{LLMs struggle to respond effectively when faced with unknown or unfamiliar situations.}
    
    In LLM \textbf{Simulation} data, LLM agents often focus on long-term goals and forget about the importance of interpersonal dynamics, leading to poor simulation results.  = LLM agents have difficulties  in social and emotional contexts
    \item \textbf{LLMs demonstrate a subpar understanding of complex human opinions and interactions.}
    \item \textbf{LLMs are deficient in accurately mirroring human behavior in social contexts.}
    \item \textbf{LLMs do not match  in their responses to subjective tasks, and they also appear to have strong preferences for certain types of tasks over others.}
    \item \textbf{LLMs do not appear to understand the consequence of their actions.}
\end{enumerate}

These limitations result in poor-quality data, which we find divergent from human counterparts, negatively affecting downstream task performance.

We stress-test challenging aspects of each data type to perform our analyses and experiments. For example, when investigating labeling data, we collect artificial labeling data for datasets in which human annotators often disagree. We also augment our findings with qualitative analyses of human and machine annotation data and qualitative feedback from human annotators.
