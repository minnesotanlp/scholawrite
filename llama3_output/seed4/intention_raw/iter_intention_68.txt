\item A call for action to creators of the data. As the prevalence of LLMs continues to grow, it becomes increasingly important to ensure that synthetic datasets are well-aligned with human attributes. This alignment is essential for ensuring that models can understand and generate language that is as close as possible to natural human language in terms of accuracy, nuance, and appropriateness. Whenever possible, we recommend using human-generated or hybrid data, such as human-annotated machine data or machine-augmented human data. When synthetic or hybrid data is used, it should reflect human language nuances, accurately representing the diversity and complexity of human language, including various dialects, sociolects, and domain-specific conventions. It should align with humans in terms of ethical standards and unbiased representations in order to minimize the perpetuation and amplification of harmful stereotypes or biases. Lastly, the closer the LLM-generated data is to human-generated data, the more useful and relevant it will be in real-world applications, such as conversation systems, content creation, and language understanding tasks. 
    
    In addition to striving to provide high quality data that is well-aligned with human needs, we urge dataset creators and curators to document their data following \citet{10.1145/3458723} and \citet{10.1145/3531146.3533231} to prioritize transparency, including detailed metadata that clearly describes the source, collection methodology, and any processing or filtering the data has undergone; to address diversity and representation by documenting the extent to which the dataset represents diverse perspectives, demographics, languages, and cultural contexts; to acknowledge and describe any potential ethical concerns related to the data, including privacy issues, consent for data use, and the potential for misuse, accompanied by guidelines for ethical use; provide guidance on the intended use cases for the dataset and its limitations; and provide a comprehensive analysis of known biases or errors within the dataset and document mitigation attempts. \citet{10.1145/3555623}'s research underlines the need for a shift from merely documenting datasets to documenting data production, emphasizing the value of designing data documentation based on the needs of data workers and viewing documentation as a boundary object across organizations. Furthermore, \citet{10.1145/3555760}'s findings indicate that current data documentation approaches are often ad hoc and myopic, underscoring the need for adaptable frameworks integrated into existing tools and workflows and automated wherever possible.
    \item Exploring \textbf{patterns of use} of datasets is crucial for evaluating LLM-generated data. We must understand how these patterns affect dataset performance and the results of the LLM.
    
    \item \textbf{LLM preferences} are another important factor to consider. Studies have shown that LLM preferences are not perfectly aligned with human preferences \cite{zheng2023judging}. This may be because LLMs have a different understanding of what makes a sentence better. 
    
    \item \textbf{Simulation} has become a significant paradigm for LLM research, with various studies using it to enhance the performance of LLMs \cite{li2023deepfake, wang2023unleashing, chan2023chateval, chen2023reconcile}. However, some of these simulations involve two LLMs arguing with each other, which results in an unstable and unpredictable output.
    
    \item In addition to the above-mentioned evaluations, we also want to use \textbf{human opinions} and \textbf{annotations} as a measure of quality, because humans are the ultimate evaluators of artificial data. 
    
    \item Moreover, LLMs are increasingly being used to generate data for all use cases like annotation, evaluation, generation, etc. In these cases, LLMs are used in a feedback loop to produce data that was originally generated by humans. This feedback loop raises interesting questions about the reliability of the data generated by LLMs. If we use LLM-generated data as a training set, how does that data affect the performance of subsequent human-generated data? Or, in other words, are LLMs simply replacing humans in data-intensive tasks? 
\end{itemize}

The advancement of large language models (LLMs) has led to their widespread adoption for complex data generation tasks once exclusive to humans, such as writing, labeling, and data evaluation. \cite{ding2022-is-gpt3-good, moller2023prompt, huang2023learning}. This proliferation, especially with models like ChatGPT, calls for detailed research to understand the capabilities and limitations of LLMs in various economically and efficiently executed data-intensive tasks. LLM-generated artificial data can match human data in performance across various benchmarks, yet the full implications and impact of its use in subsequent tasks remain elusive.
