\paragraph{Second Order Experiment}
We train two RoBERTa reward models, one on human preferences and the other on machine preferences, for each dataset. We then test each model on the human reward test set. Our results (Table!\ref{tab:preference:human}) show that the models trained on machine preferences, when compared to those trained on human preferences, have about $10\%$ lower accuracy on the human test set for both datasets. This indicates that machine preferences are not perfectly aligned with human preferences. Further, the highest accuracy overrall is achieved by a machine-trained model on a machine test set, indicating that machine preferences may be easier for models to learn than human preferences. This may be due to machine preferences correlating more strongly with signals such as lexical features and entailment, as discussed above.
