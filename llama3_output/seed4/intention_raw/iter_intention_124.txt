\subsection{Main findings}
%\noindent \textbf{
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep,topsep=5pt,leftmargin=*]
\item We find several cases where artificial data diverges from human data. The divergence in LLM performance is frequently attributed to their limited, superficial comprehension of tasks, as evidenced by their consistent overrepresentation of majority opinions across all datasets, including those with highly subjective content. Additionally, LLMs struggle to align with minority viewpoints and exhibit less variability in annotations for subjective tasks than humans, indicating a lack of understanding about the controversial nature of certain labeling tasks and a tendency to overconfidently mirror only majority responses. 
    
\item LLMs, when tasked with generating text for specific contexts, tend to apply textual styles unnaturally and simplistically, as observed in scenarios like composing 'positive Tweets,' where they predominantly use \textit{joy}, despite real human examples exhibiting a broader range of emotions. Moreover, unlike humans who adapt discourse patterns to different domains, LLMs often recycle the same patterns across various contexts, suggesting a superficial grasp of the interplay between discourse, textual style, and context.
    
\item Our findings indicate that LLMs demonstrate a shallow approach to preference tasks, often basing decisions on the most salient linguistic cues in a sentence, in stark contrast to humans, who exhibit only a minor correlation and seemingly consider additional factors.
    
\item In addition to these limitations, we note that LLM overconfidence \anonymous{need citation on overconfidence} also impacts artificial data. Our manual annotations of artificial instruction sets find that artificial instructions contain many errors, particularly for closed questions. Our manual investigation of simulation data reveals that LLMs also employ a superficial approach to problem-solving, often focusing on the most salient information and failing to consider the broader context of the problem.
