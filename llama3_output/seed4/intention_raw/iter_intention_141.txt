\subsection{About this Data Type}\label{sec:instruct-background}
Instruction prompts are used to refine the performance of LLMs, particularly for understanding and executing complex instructions. The purpose is to make LLMs more adept at handling diverse and unpredictable user requests. However, creating datasets of instructions is challenging, because it is difficult to come up with a comprehensive set of complex and diverse instructions. For this reason, multiple synthetic instruction prompt resources have been created. 

\subsection{Critique of Related Work}\label{sec:instruct-related}
Numerous studies have leveraged the capability of LLMs to generate synthetic instruction datasets for fine-tuning LLMs \cite{alpaca, honovich2022unnatural, wang-etal-2022-super, peng2023instruction}. In generating synthetic instruction datasets, models must be able to generate diverse tasks and adhere to the specified format of instruction, input, and output. However, these synthetic datasets often mirror the styles and outputs of their parent LLM, with a lack of comprehensive documentation about what task types are included. In contrast, human-crafted datasets \cite{2020unifiedqa, muennighoff2022crosslingual,lai2023chatgpt, sanh2021multitask, mishra2021natural, supernaturalinstructions, DatabricksBlog2023DollyV2} show a wider range of distinct task types. We aim to further understand the \textit{task diversity in synthetically created instruction datasets}. 

The second issue is the inclination of LLMs often focus on superficial patterns and output formats during instruction tuning \cite{kung-peng-2023-models}. This tendency is problematic as it can lead to a form of overfitting, where the models are adept at handling types of tasks they are frequently exposed to (like generative or Open-QA tasks) but struggle with tasks that are not well-represented in the fine-tuning data \cite{gudibande2023false}. This suggests a need for a robust collection of instruction-input-output patterns, although current trends lean towards generative or open-QA tasks with limited formal diversity.

Lastly, the presence of high error rates in instructions (\citet{honovich2022unnatural} and \citet{wang2023selfinstruct} both report error rates close to 50\% for their respective instruction datasets) further complicates the effectiveness of these models. These errors can have a compounding effect on the model's performance, especially in downstream tasks. When LLMs are fine-tuned on data with high error rates, they may not only replicate these errors but also struggle to correctly interpret and execute tasks, leading to a reduction in overall effectiveness and reliability.

These three issues show that there is a need to evaluate the quality and diversity of synthetic instruction datasets more rigorously. The tendency of synthetic datasets to mirror the limited styles and outputs of their parent LLMs indicates a gap in the variety and complexity of tasks they present. This limitation is further exacerbated by the LLMs' focus on superficial patterns and output formats, which can lead to a narrow scope of learning and adaptability in handling diverse real-world scenarios. Additionally, the high error rates in instructions generated by LLMs raise concerns about the reliability and accuracy of the models trained on these datasets. 

\subsection{Datasets}\label{sec:instruct-data}
We examine four synthetic instruction datasets, including \textsc{Unnatural Instructions} \cite{honovich2022unnatural}, \textsc{Self-Instruct} \cite{wang2023selfinstruct}, \textsc{Alpaca-Cleaned} \cite{alpaca}, and \textsc{GPT-4-LLM} \cite{peng2023instruction}.  We also rely on four human-written instruction datasets for comparison and evaluation, including \textsc{Flan} \cite{weifinetuned}, \textsc{Dolly} \cite{DatabricksBlog2023DollyV2}, \textsc{Supernatural Instructions} \cite{supernaturalinstructions}, and \textsc{Instructions in the Wild} \cite{instructionwild}.

\noindent \textbf{Rationale}: We selected these datasets to capture the variety of instruction types and domains. We used human-generated datasets for comparison and evaluation of models fine-tuned on synthetic instruction data to show the downstream effects of different types of fine-tuning data.

\paragraph{Experiment Setup} To understand some of the characteristics of synthetic instruction datasets, we annotated 200 samples each of Unnatural Instructions, Self-Instruct, Alpaca-Cleaned, and GPT-4-LLM (a total of 800 samples). Our annotation process focused on whether the instruction takes the form of closed versus open questions and answering; and the prevalence of errors and distribution of error types. Our \textsc{Closed-QA} samples are defined as tasks with specific, objective answers, using given input or specific criteria. On the other hand, \textsc{Open-QA} samples are defined as more exploratory, subjective, or creative tasks. They typically involve broader questions or tasks with multiple correct answers or approaches.

We also performed an error analysis on all 800 samples. For this analysis, we considered four \anonymous{error labels? } labels: (1) \textsc{Incomprehensible Instruction} (i.e., impossible to follow based on the provided input); (2) \textsc{Inconsistent Input} (i.e., the input does not support or contradicts the instruction); (3) \textsc{Incorrect Output} (i.e., the output is an incorrect solution given the instruction and input); and (4) \textsc{Inconsistent Output} (i.e., the output is formally inconsistent with the expected task description.)

\paragraph{Metrics} We performed an error frequency analysis to quantify the occurrence of each error type in both Closed-QA and Open-QA samples. By calculating the frequency of each error type, we can determine the prevalence of errors in each category and compare the distribution across closed-QA and open-QA tasks.

\paragraph{Result Analysis}

Our analysis shows a divide in our four synthetic datasets in terms of broad instruction types and error rates. Unnatural Instructions and Self-Instruct contain more closed-QA type questions, while Cleaned Alpaca and GPT-4-LLM contain mostly Open-QA-type questions. Across Self-Instruct, Cleaned Alpaca, and GPT-4-LLM, the ratio of correct to incorrect samples is lower for the closed-QA type instructions (Unnatural Instructions has an insignificant number of Open-QA type questions to begin with). This shows a bias in the synthetic data against high-quality Closed-QA type questions. The concern for downstream models is that the lower quality of Closed-QA type instructions in these datasets could hinder the effectiveness of downstream models trained on them for structured, fact-based queries. Since Closed-QA tasks often require precise and accurate answers, the high error rates in these types of questions might limit the ability of models to perform well. This raises concerns about the generalizability of models trained on these datasets. If models are primarily exposed to OpenQA tasks, they may develop a skewed understanding of what constitutes a correct answer, potentially leading to reduced performance on more structured and objective tasks.
