"\textbf{Task Labels}: Majority voting is a standard and widely used method for deriving single ground truth labels from multiple annotations. In many applications, especially those with subjective nature, this leads to skewed behavior and poor representation of minority opinions. \textbf{Mitigation}: Diversity can be enhanced by using multiple metrics in annotation, such as a majority and a minority label. The \textbf{soft labeling} approach also uses a single label, but assigns higher confidence scores to the minority label. \textbf{Noise Inference}: Noise in annotations, particularly in subjective tasks like sentiment and controversy detection, can be amplified when aggregated. Methods like \textit{average} and \textit{standard deviation} are widely used to quantify this noise. However, in ordinal data, where differences between levels aren't uniform as in interval or ratio data, average and standard deviation still serve as standard measures. \textbf{Disagreement Analysis of the Human and Machine Annotations} To explore \textit{variation} in annotations in the inter-model setup, we contrast the variation of the human annotations group with the variation of the intra-model annotations of the machines. 
    In ordinal data, where differences between levels aren't uniform as in interval or ratio data, other metrics like \textit{standard deviation} or \textit{variance} are still useful for gauging the spread of opinions. We also use the \textit{Wasserstein distance}, also known as the Earth Mover’s distance, for a more nuanced understanding of the variation between human and machine annotations. This metric is particularly useful for ordinal data. This metric is also used in the loss function of our proposed dataset, \textsc{TenPageStories}.

\textbf{Label Distribution}: We also look into the \textit{distributional differences} between the majority and minority annotations. We use a \textit{heat map} to visualize the label distribution of the majority and minority annotations. We also use the \textit{alpaca} library in R to compare the distribution of the human annotations group with the distribution of the intra-model annotations of the machines.
