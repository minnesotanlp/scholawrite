\begin{abstract}
\small  Our research explores the impact of generating data for diverse, nuanced tasks. 
In particular, we find that large language models are prone to developing hallucinations, or inaccuracies in the data generated by the model. 
These hallucinations  in the form of hallucinated tasks, often overlooked in the data generated by LLMs. 
We also find that these hallucinations are amplified when the model is trained on the data generated by itself, which can limit the effectiveness of models trained on this type of data. 
We propose that researchers consider the nature of the task and the likelihood of hallucination in order to use LLM-generated data effectively, or in order to mitigate the effect of hallucinations in the data generated by their models. 
We also find that a crucial oversight of previous work is the lack of a systematic study of the task types
