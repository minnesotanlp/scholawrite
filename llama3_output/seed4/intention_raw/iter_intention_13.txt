\item \textsc{Wasserstein distance}: This metric compares probability distributions, especially with ordinal values. It is also called Earth Mover’s distance because it can be interpreted informally as the distance it would move a ball down an hill.
    
\end{enumerate}
\paragraph{Experiment Setup}
We use the  \textsc{Scarecrow} dataset \citep{guo2023close} and its human-written counterpart, \textsc{Deepfake}. The \textsc{Scarecrow} dataset consists of 8,256 sample of paired human-machine texts from the \textsc{Workers} dataset, relevant for its social media content. Each pair consists of (1) a pair of texts from the LLM and (2) the human-written counterpart of that pair. The following year, the \textsc{Scarecrow} dataset was expanded to include 50,000 samples of new social media content, generated by 50,000 new LLM "workers." This expansion was motivated in part by the success of the LLM-generated data in the previous year's evaluation, as well as the desire to further understand the nature of LLM-generated data. 

\paragraph{Metrics} Out of the paired human-LLM texts, we count how often the label \textsc{flips}, i.e. differs between the original human and the resulting machine text, for each category of high-level attributes. Since high-level textual attributes are highly domain-dependent (e.g., formality is often present in news or scientific articles but rarely present in tweets), we focus on examining the flips for each domain of our datasets. 

\paragraph{Result} \label{sec:figurative-results}

Our findings (see Fig~\ref{fig:high_level_features_radar}) highlight notable differences between human and LLM-generated texts. We primarily observe that texts from LLMs tend to be formal and employ more metaphoric language. More details are presented in Table \label{table:formality_qual} in Appendix \ref{sec:appendix:free}.

Furthermore, we noted divergences between human and LLM texts in terms of specific social-dynamics-related attributes. For instance, human texts exhibit strong anger in situations involving a social power dynamic and use irony during a more light-hearted social setting. In contrast, LLM texts tend to introduce more joy into positive Tweets, more irony in negative ones, and add neutrality into texts related to social conflicts.  Our results indicate that the resulting texts often show a different spread of high-level attributes compared to the human-authored counterparts, even though models can generate texts that align with the given social dimension labels.
