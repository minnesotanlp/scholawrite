We identify consistent biases in LLM-generated data and outline strategies for mitigation. Representational bias in LLMs manifests as a preference for majority opinions, often at the expense of minority views. Additionally, LLMs exhibit primary and recency biases, focusing on certain aspects of data and affecting task specificity and performance. This is evident in synthetic instruction datasets, where LLMs favor open-ended questions over structured tasks, raising concerns about their ability to generalize across different query types. LLMs also differ from human patterns in processing emotional content and semantic relationships.

We find that artificial data has strong weaknesses that might be attributed to the curse of generality for LMs: artificial data is notably less human-like in situations in which general, common, or well-accepted outputs are not possible. In labeling tasks, LLMs do not represent minority views well and show strong preferences for majority opinions. When creating instruction datasets, humans include many specific closed questions that have a highly constrained correct answer; LLMs strongly favor writing open-ended questions that are easier to answer correctly, rather than writing closed questions in which only a single answer could be correct.
