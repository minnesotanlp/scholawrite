%- research gap and methods we use 

We survey each type of LLM-generated data and explore challenging aspects () of each data type to perform our analyses and experiments. Across all these types of LLM-generated data, we utilize a comprehensive set of quantitative and qualitative metrics, including second-order effect analysis, to thoroughly evaluate the reliability and practicality of the data produced by LLMs.


Table \ref{tab:transposed_related_summary} summarizes the five distinct data types in the landscape of LLM-generated data, each with its unique set of challenges and research gaps that we unearth from previous work. Free-form text data often blurs the line between human and LLM outputs, with previous work focusing on detailed analysis of linguistic features. Our stress testing approach includes artifact analysis of high-level attributes, label flipping, and contrasting human and LLM discourse patterns for effective stress testing. We also investigate whether artifacts impact the performance of classifiers trained on the text. Task label data, heavily influenced by majority annotations, typically lacks diversity in opinions, particularly in subjective tasks. We tackle this by comparing label distributions between human and LLM outputs and conducting variation analysis. We also observe the amplification of existing artifacts through our second-order effect analysis. Instruction-based data from LLMs, which usually lacks diversity in out-of-distribution tasks, presents high error rates. Addressing this, we employ human validation, error distribution analysis, and second-order effect analysis. In the case of preference data, used as a proxy for human preferences, we assess the distributional differences between human and LLM preferences. Lastly, we analyze challenges such as unexpected agent behaviors in the lesser-studied domain of simulation data. These are scrutinized through artifact and correlation analysis, coupled with human validation.

% overall themes 
Through our investigation into the five distinct LLM-generated data types, we notice thematic areas in which LLM-generated data falls short of its human counterpart:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{LLMs struggle to respond effectively when faced with unknown or unfamiliar situations.}
    
    \item \textbf{LLMs exhibit strong preferences for being correct, rather than objective.}
    
    \item \textbf{LLMs are deficient in accurately mirroring human behavior during social interactions.}
