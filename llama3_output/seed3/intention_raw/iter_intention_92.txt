\subsubsection{Discourse Analysis}\label{sec:free-text-discourse}
\begin{wrapfigure}[7]{R}[0.5cm]{2.8in}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth,height=\textheight,keepaspectratio]{images/free_text/three_interesting_m3_motifs_for_artifact_paper.pdf}
\caption{Example network motifs of size three.}
\label{fig:free_text/three_interesting_m3_motifs_for_artifact_paper}
\end{wrapfigure}
We apply an existing discourse parser \citep{liu-etal-2021-dmrst} to the paired human-machine texts to perform the discourse analysis. For a given document, the parser produces a hierarchical discourse tree that adheres to a discourse framework called the Rhetorical Structure Theory \citep{mann1987rhetorical}. We note that this hierarchical tree can be seen as a recursive hypergraph in graph theory. By converting the trees to hypergraphs, we can conduct some standard graph analyses such as computing the distribution of network motifs of varying sizes. These network motifs are small directed subgraphs with edges labeled with a discourse relation and can be considered as a building block or a ``vocabulary'' of larger graphs. For example, Figure \ref{fig:free_text/three_interesting_m3_motifs_for_artifact_paper} illustrates three motifs of size three that distinctively occur in either the machine or human side of texts, where an edge is a label of discourse relation, and a node is either an elementary discourse unit or a hyperedge node, comprising multiple subgraphs. We check the differences in these motif distributions and validate if they represent any meaningful patterns in discourse structures and, thus, in the ways the documents are written or generated. (For details on these motifs, see Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix:free}.) 
We examine each document graph $G$ in a domain by counting the occurrence of a particular motif or subgraph $M$ in $G$, and then normalize these counts based on the size of the graph (i.e., the number of edges $|E|$). This is aggregated over all instances and averaged across domains.

\textbf{Findings}
Our discourse results, shown in Figure~\ref{fig:motif_hist_dist-norm_by_edges}, show distinct differences in motif distributions depending on their domain. In general, the more formal the domain, the texts show more motifs that contain converging hyperedges that denote the linking of a lower representation to a higher one (i.e., phrases to sentences, sentences to paragraphs, etc.). We note that this is not a perfect correspondence since hyperedges represent not only textual relations but also other  discourse relations. 
For ``Yelp'' domain, we observe human-written texts talk more about ``Temporal'' and ``Evaluation'' relations while machine-written ones talk more about the general ``Background'' of a place. This suggests that domain-specific distinctiveness can arise from human-authored texts while machine-generated texts seem relatively general. 
A similar pattern is observed for Figure \ref{fig:free_text/dist_diff_five_domains} in Appendix \ref{sec:appendix:free} which shows distributional differences between human- and machine-generated document graphs for a larger number of motifs.
