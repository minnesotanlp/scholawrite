The advancement of large language models (LLMs) has led to their widespread adoption for complex data generation tasks once exclusive to humans, such as writing, labeling, and data evaluation. \cite{ding2022-is-gpt3-good, moller2023prompt, huang2023learning}. This proliferation, especially with models like ChatGPT, calls for detailed research to understand the capabilities and limitations of LLMs in various economically and efficiently executed data-intensive tasks. LLM-generated artificial data can match human data in performance across various benchmarks, yet the full implications and impact of its use in subsequent tasks remain elusive.

% risks of LLM-generated data ; no one is talking about the risks (1st order effect)% risk of artificial(artificial))intelligence
Limited research has highlighted the disparity between LLM-generated labels and human annotations \cite{reiss2023testing}, prompting calls for cross-domain evaluation against human data. There is no consensus on how artificial data differs from human data because it is a fairly new and highly fluent source of data 
\cite{sadasivan2023can}. As illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:llm_world}, LLMs are now being used to generate data for all use cases like annotation, evaluation, generation, etc. Artificial data generated from one LLM could be used for one or more use cases, leading to the rise of an LLM ecosystem. Recent studies, notably those by \citet{shumailov2023curse} and \citet{briesch2023large}, have highlighted concerns regarding the quality and diversity of artificial data employed in, and subsequently produced by, training loops. The growing dependence on such data for its convenience and lower costs and speculations of GPT-5 training on its own generated data \footnote{Link to tweet: \url{https://shorturl.at/kmAY2}} raise concerns about an emerging cycle of AI-reliant solely on artificial inputs.
