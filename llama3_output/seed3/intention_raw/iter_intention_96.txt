The advancement of large language models (LLMs) has led to their widespread adoption for complex data generation tasks once exclusive to humans, such as writing, labeling, and data evaluation. \cite{ding2022-is-gpt3-good, moller2023prompt, huang2023learning}. This proliferation, especially with models like ChatGPT, calls for detailed research to understand the capabilities and limitations of LLMs in various economically and efficiently executed data-intensive tasks. LLM-generated artificial data can match human data in performance across various benchmarks, yet the full implications and impact of its use in subsequent tasks remain elusive.

To forward the understanding of artificial data and its effects, the research questions we investigate are - 
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{RQ1} What is the nature of SOTA artificial data? How does it compare to human data, and what artifacts does it contain?
\item \textbf{RQ2} How specific are the artifacts of artificial data to certain types of data produced by large language models (LLMs), and how much do they apply to all types of data generated by LLMs?
\item \textbf{RQ3} Does training on artificial data compromise performance outcomes compared to similar human-generated data? 

% our different kinds of data
This work contributes to this understanding by taking a broad inventory of artificial data, possible ``artifacts'' or divergences from the corresponding human data, and the effects of using this data. We prioritize breadth, covering five different data types, rather than depth, to understand overarching themes in synthetic data that transcend domain-specific scenarios.
The five types of artificial data we examine are :
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Free Text} written by an LLM, often used to compensate for data scarcity issues.
\item \textbf{Task Labels} for classification tasks over textual input, bypassing the need for human annotators.
\item \textbf{Instruction prompts} written by LLMs for instruction fine-tuning, eliminating the need for humans to write comprehensive sets of diverse instructions.
\item \textbf{Preference}, specifically evaluating which text is better, is useful for human alignment tasks such as training reward models used in RLHF.
\item \textbf{Simulation}, in which two LLM agents engage in a conversation, is a relatively underexplored paradigm, but early research suggests use cases in improving model performance and modeling complex social interactions. \anonymous{It's a little odd that this is a full sentence while the others aren't. We categorize LLM-generated text data based on the level of output constraint, ranging from tightly constrained (such as task labels data, which consists of discrete labels for a task) to lightly constrained (like free text data, where output is freely generated from an input). Examples of each data type are further illustrated in Figure ~\ref{fig:figure1}. This spectrum captures the wide-ranging capabilities and applications of large language models, each representing a specific type of output and process integral to the functioning of LLMs. This classification helps understand how each data type contributes to the broader AI and machine learning development landscape.

To address RQ1, we examine \textit{first-order effects} for each data type. We define first-order effects as about the data itself, i.e. any differences or inconsistencies between the artificial data and the corresponding human data. To address RQ2, we examine \textit{second-order effects}, which we define as any differences or inconsistencies in the outcomes of NLP pipelines containing artificial data and the outcomes from corresponding pipelines containing the corresponding human data. We address RQ3 through an overview of the results of all first- and second-order experiments.
