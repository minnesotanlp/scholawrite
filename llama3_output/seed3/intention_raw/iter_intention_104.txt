\subsection{Second-order Analysis}\label{sec:task-second}
\paragraph{Hypothesis}
We train an LLM on both human and machine-generated annotations to identify potential second-order effects and biases. We hypothesize that biases would become more pronounced over time with a concentrated distribution highlighting specific stereotypes and assumptions. Additionally, we expect more entropy over time among the annotations generated by inter or intra-model LLMs because of how model learn. 
We know that models are eager learners in that their weights will change easily during training or fine-tuning \anonymous{does this need citing?}. Humans, on the other hand, are governed by societal or self-rules that make us more resistant to change. Therefore, we expect a model trained on LLM-generated annotations to change its annotation decisions more easily (label-flipping) compared to a model trained on human-generated annotations. The more stable nature of human annotations can help us retain more diversity over time.
\paragraph{Experiment Setup}
We fine-tune a multi-headed RoBERTA-base classifier \cite{liu2019roberta} on both the human and machine-generated annotations. The artificial data was generated by models mentioned in Section \ref{sec:task-first}. Before fine-tuning, we order the training data annotations based on frequency (the more popular opinions are placed first). We do this on both a dataset-wide scale and individual-level scale \cite{jung2023clusterguided}. We use both because performance differences will depend on the data - in other words, whether knowing the majority for other instances in the dataset will help the annotation process.
