Figure \ref{fig:figure2} demonstrates the variety of techniques applied for stress testing artificial data, as detailed in earlier sections, while Table \ref{tab:stress_testing_overview} provides an overview of the methodologies employed for each data type. Notably, approaches like \textit{distributional difference} prove effective for contrasting almost all categories of LLM-generated data with human-generated data. Methods such as \textit{label flipping} and \textit{correlation analysis} are widely utilized across numerous data types. Except for Simulation data, which is still in the nascent stages of exploration and requires more first-order analysis, second-order effect analysis is conducted for all data types.

\noindent \textbf{Main findings}
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep,topsep=5pt,leftmargin=*]
\item We find several cases where artificial data diverges from human data. The divergence in LLM performance is frequently attributed to their limited, superficial comprehension of tasks, as evidenced by their consistent overrepresentation of majority opinions across all datasets, including those with highly subjective content. Additionally, LLMs struggle to align with minority viewpoints and exhibit less variability in annotations for subjective tasks than humans, indicating a lack of understanding about the controversial nature of certain labeling tasks and a tendency to overconfidently mirror only majority responses. 
    
\item LLMs also demonstrate a subpar understanding of complex human opinions and interactions, as they often simplify situations and overlook the nuances of certain tasks. For instance, LLMs deciding which sentence has a stronger positive sentiment make decisions that are tightly coupled with the number of standalone positive tokens in a sentence, in contrast to humans, who take a more nuanced approach. 
    
\item Our findings indicate that LLMs are prone to produce data that is uncorrelated with the intended use of the data, leading to a stronger need to use alternative evaluation methods when relying on artificial data.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:sec10}
%\anonymous{ tie back to motivation, talk about the artificial future and how it will be a lot harder to distinguish }
In conclusion, our comprehensive analysis reveals that LLM-generated data exhibits significant discrepancies compared to human-generated data. These discrepancies are evident as second-order effects in LLM-generated data, underscoring the importance of addressing the artifacts inherent in LLM-generated data. We propose several mitigation strategies, such as employing human validation and incorporating domain-specific information, to enhance the quality of LLM-generated data. Furthermore, we concur with the observation that LLMs tend to overrepresent majority opinions and underrepresent minority views. This tendency is exacerbated by the limited, superficial comprehension of LLMs when encountering subjective tasks, leading to a decrease in the diversity of their responses. By pointing out these issues, we intend to stimulate further research to enhance the reliability of LLM-generated data.
