\subsection{Mitigating Biases from LLM Generated Task Labels}\label{sec:task-mitigation}
\anonymous{outline very specific actionable suggestions}
Majority voting and averaging, which are standard methods for deriving single ground truth labels from multiple annotations, often fail to capture the systematic disagreements among annotators. This is especially the case in subjective tasks like detecting sentiment, aggression, and hate speech. Through our experiments, we find that LLMs tend to under-represent minority opinions. This risks marginalizing minority perspectives, potentially reinforcing societal disparities \cite{prabhakaran2021releasing}. This raises a dire need to preserve disagreement in LLM annotations so that they more accurately mirror human annotations. \citeauthor{lee2023large} recently explored Monte Carlo Estimation (MCE) and Log Probability Estimation (LPE) as ways to find the distributional difference between human and LLM task labels. Other methods include using Bayesian methods \cite{paun2018comparing}, integrating disagreements into loss functions \cite{plank2014learning}, and modeling several annotators’ labels as multiple tasks \cite{davani2022dealing}. 

There is also a rising need for validation using human datasets \cite{pangakis2023automated}. Several studies testing LLMs on widely used datasets potentially suffer from data contamination, where LLMs may memorize information, leading to misleadingly strong performance that may not extend to new datasets and tasks, highlighting the need for clear guidelines to ensure effective and accurate use of these tools. \anonymous{citations needed}
