\subsection{Second-order Analysis}\label{sec:task-second}
\paragraph{Hypothesis}
We train an LLM on human and machine-generated annotations to identify potential second-order effects and biases. The following section investigates the hypothesis that biases become more pronounced over time, with a \anonymous{this is unclear}concentrated distribution highlighting specific stereotypes and assumptions.

\paragraph{Experiment Setup}
We fine-tune a multi-headed RoBERTa-base classifier \cite{liu2019roberta} on both the human and machine-generated annotations. The artificial data was generated by models mentioned in Section \ref{sec:task-first}. Before fine-tuning, we order the training data annotations in four ways. We create two versions based on frequency (the more popular opinions are placed first). We do this on both a dataset-wide scale and individual-level scale \cite{jung2023clusterguided}. We use both because performance differences will depend on the data - in other words, whether knowing the majority for other instances in the dataset will help the annotation process. Another version is based on numerical sorting and the last is shuffled.
