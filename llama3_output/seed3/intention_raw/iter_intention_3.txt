The advancement of large language models (LLMs) has led to their widespread adoption for complex data generation tasks once exclusive to humans, such as writing, labeling, and data evaluation. This proliferation, especially with models like ChatGPT, calls for detailed research to understand the capabilities and limitations of LLMs in various economically and efficiently executed data-intensive tasks. LLM-generated artificial data can match human data in performance across various benchmarks, yet the full implications and impact of its use in subsequent tasks remain elusive. In most instances, the prevailing notion that LLM-generated data is comparable to human data may scratch the surface, as this perspective primarily addresses less complex tasks. Our theory suggests a deeper complexity, illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:iceberg}, where LLM-generated content likely fails to capture the nuances of human data, particularly minority views, intricate preferences, or specialized instructions. Moreover, high-level human attributes such as humor and sarcasm are often absent in LLM-generated texts, and the structural nuances expected in human writing are not fully replicated, with simulated LLM conversations markedly diverging from natural human dialogue.

This work contributes to this understanding by taking a broad inventory of artificial data, possible ``artifacts'' or divergences from the corresponding human data, and the effects of adapting this data. We prioritize a wide breadth, covering six different data types, rather than depth, to understand overarching themes in synthetic data that transcend domain-specific scenarios.

The six types of artificial data we examine are
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Text} written by an LLM, often used to compensate for data scarcity issues.
\item \textbf{Labels} for classification tasks over textual input, bypassing the need for human annotators.
\item \textbf{Instruction prompts} written by LLMs for use in instruction fine-tuning, eliminating the need for humans to write comprehensive sets of diverse instructions.
\item \textbf{Preference}, or the evaluation of which text is better, useful for human alignment tasks such as training reward models used in RLHF.
\item \textbf{Simulation}, in which two LLM agents engage in a conversation, is a relatively underexplored paradigm, but early research suggests use cases in improving model performance and modeling complex social interactions.  
\end{itemize}

Figure~\ref{fig:figure1} showcases examples of six distinct artificial data types, representing a substantial and varied subset of potential artificial data generated by large language models. This categorization not only reflects the extensive range of artificial data types available but also aids in understanding the unique contribution of each data type to the wider field of AI and machine learning development.

To forward the understanding of artificial data and its effects, the research questions we investigate are

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{RQ1} What is the nature of SOTA artificial data? How does it compare to human data, and what artifacts does it contain?
\item \textbf{RQ2} Do artificial data negatively affect downstream outcomes, and if so, how? How can we mitigate any of these effects?
\item \textbf{RQ3} To what extent are artifacts of artificial data specific to individual domains/data types, and to what extent are they general?
\end{itemize}
\anonymous{Why first and second order effect?}
To address RQ1, we examine \textit{first-order effects} for each data type. We define first-order effects as about the data itself, i.e. any differences or inconsistencies between the artificial data and the corresponding human data. To address RQ2, we examine \textit{second-order effects}, which we define as about the downstream result of using the artificial data in some NLP pipeline, i.e., any differences or inconsistencies in the outcomes of NLP pipelines containing artificial data and the outcomes from corresponding pipelines containing the corresponding human data. We address RQ3 through an overview of the results of all first- and second-order experiments.
