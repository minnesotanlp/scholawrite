�atically, we find that the use of Large Language Models (LLMs) in graph-structured data generation, although capable of producing realistic outputs, often loses the nuanced understanding of subtle graph-structured information. This suggests that LLMs may not fully replace the importance of human intuition and judgment in interpreting complex graph structures. 

\textbf{Experiment setup} We examine four synthetic datasets including \textsc{HC3} \cite{guo2023close}, \textsc{Scarecrow} \cite{dou-etal-2022-gpt}, \textsc{Deepfake} \cite{li2023deepfake}, and \textsc{Workers} \cite{moller2023prompt}. These datasets are characterized by their different domains and their potential for containing role-flipping, which we define as the flipping of the agent's assigned role. We hypothesize that role-flipping can be more easily observed in datasets with a longer input length and more complex dynamics. 

\textsc{Scarecrow} \cite{dou-etal-2022-gpt} is a collection of reviews from various online stores with different order of operations. We filter the data by the length of the generated texts and keep only the conversations with the length of 8000 tokens or more. 

\textsc{Workers} \cite{moller2023prompt} is a collection of conversations between two LLM agents identified as \textsc{Agent 1} and \textsc{Agent 2} with different personas. Different personas are used to generate different personas, leading to role-flipping in the conversations. We first generate conversations with the length of 8000 tokens or more, following the same generation setup as Scarecrow. 

\textsc{Simulation} \cite{li2023deepfake} is a collection of conversations between two LLM agents identified as \textsc{Agent 1} and \textsc{Agent 2} with the persona  with the persona  and occupation. The personas were provided to the agents in the conversation, and the conversation continued until one of the agents interrupted the conversation, claiming to be confused, and the conversation was marked as role-flipped. We used this method to detect the role-flipped conversations. 

\textit{Role-flipping} is a relatively uncommon phenomenon in the simulated conversation, and it only happens on the \textsc{CAMEL AI-World} dataset. 
\textbf{Conversation Length and Occupation} 
We first found that the conversations with role-flipping are significantly longer than the average conversation in the dataset, which can be also observed in \textsc{CAMEL AI-World}. 
Moreover, we found that the role-flipping rate can be different depending on the occupations that the agents are playing in the conversation. 
Specifically, the role-flipping rate is much higher for conversations with a combination of occupations that are more difficult for the LLM to handle, such as the combination of Positive and Informal. 
On the other hand, the role-flipping rate is much lower for conversations with a combination of occupations that are easier for the LLM to handle, such as the combination of Positive and Formal. 
We also found that role-flipping rarely occurs in the first 6 messages in a conversation. 
Role-flipping almost never occurs in the first two messages of a conversation. 
When the first message in a conversation has been given, it is almost impossible for the role-flipping to occur in the following messages. 
This suggests that role-flipping may be attributed to the "digression" in the conversation, and the messages that cause role-flipping are those that are not related to the conversation's context.
