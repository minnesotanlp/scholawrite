\paragraph{Behavior of Uncontrolled Styles}
We examine how generations from the style-controlled models differ from the original model with respect to the \textit{uncontrolled} styles, as evaluated by our style discriminators. Certain textual styles are highly related, e.g. \textit{positive} and \textit{joy}. Correspondingly, as expected, models with the target style positive show a corresponding increase in the joy of their generations. 

However, we also note that the 1-style informal model shows an increase in joy that is comparable to the joy increase of the positive models. To find whether there is a relationship between informality and joy, we check the original data's full texts and the base model's generations for correlations between joy and informality scores. We found they were not highly correlated (Pearson's $r = 0.1$, $p < 0.05$; this also replicates earlier results from \cite{kang2021style}). This implies that this result cannot be attributed to any inherent association between the styles, and it is possible that steering generations towards a target style may impact the prevalence of non-target styles as well.
