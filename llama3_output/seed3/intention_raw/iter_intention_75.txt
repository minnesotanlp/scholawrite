\paragraph{Hypothesis} We hypothesize that the difference in linguistic features between two sentences shows varying distribution between human and LLM preference labels of ``tie'' (i.e., \textit{no preference between the two sentences}). 

\paragraph{Experiment Setup} 

Among the preference labels available in \textsc{p2c}, we used \textit{subjective preference} that has been gathered by the responses from crowd workers by asking them the following prompt: \textit{Between A and B, which sentence is more positive (neutral, or negative)?} For our analysis, we used the prompt \textit{Could you suggest  the sentence that is more positive (neutral, or negative) between the two sentences?} and showed the ranking of  the lexicon-based preference in the following  each sentence of a pair, and then compared the correlation between the lexicon-based preference and each of GPT-3 and human preferences.

\subsubsection{First Order Effects}\label{sec:pref-first-results}

\paragraph{Semantic Similarity} Overall, we observe differences in the distribution of semantic textual similarity between human and LLM preference labels. According to Figures \ref{fig:pref:p2c-first-semantic}, \ref{fig:pref:cobbler-first-semantic}, \ref{fig:pref:mtbench-first-semantic} in Appendix \ref{appendix:pref}), it can be observed that the distributions of ``tie'' preference from GPTs tend to differ from that of ``non-tie''. Except for some parts of \textsc{cobbler}, humans show distributions of ``tie'' and ``non-tie'' preferences almost overlapped or balanced. We suppose that the algorithmic decision-making process of LLMs tends to be more caught by the semantic similarity of paired sentences, while humans tend to be not sensitive to the semantic differences while making decisions. 

We also compare the average preference label distribution between human and LLMs, based on different linguistic features of a pair of sentences during evaluation. Note that negative x-axis values indicate the second sentence in a pairwise setup has a larger value of that certain feature, or the first sentence if x-axis values become positive. On the other hand, negative y-axis values mean the second sentence has been preferred, or the first sentence if y-axis values become positive. 


In terms of the linguistic features (perplexity and toxicity), we commonly observe that GPTs show a slightly similar behavior trend with humans across all varying features. One possible explanation is that, considering RLHF process that trains GPTs based on human preference, instruction-tuned LLMs learned to \textbf{mimic human behavior heuristically} by choosing simpler sentences and not being sensitive to different levels of toxicity between two sentences during evaluation.
