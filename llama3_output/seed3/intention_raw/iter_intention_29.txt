νεφo With the increase in the generation capabilities of large language models (LLMs), it has become increasingly desirable to include fine-grained control of the styles included in the model's output. For instance, in almost all cases, toxicity and hate speech must be tightly controlled such that the model does not produce harmful output. At the same time, in response to the user's preferences or the application, it can be beneficial for the LLM to simultaneously control additional attributes such as humor, formality, or the use of figurative language. In order to achieve these goals reliably, techniques for robust multi-style control are needed. We also find that controlling for one style can lead to side effect changes in other styles, further motivating the need for multi-style control.

State-of-the-art RLHF models such as GPT-4 are adept at producing text according to some style combinations, but struggle with others our experiments indicate that without sophisticated prompting strategies, GPT-4 struggles to control for multiple styles at once. 



In this work, we propose a straightforward and relatively lightweight approach to controlling for multiple styles simultaneously: fine-tuning a language model using proximal policy optimization (PPO) with a dynamically weighted combination of rewards for each style. We experiment with several methods to compose the reward, and we find that this multi-style objective works best with a dynamically weighted combination of the outputs from each target style's discriminator. We also find that simple steps such as confidence calibration of discriminator output can improve the model performance.
We also create a plug-and-play baseline for comparison, expanding PPLM \citep{Dathathri2020Plug} to control multiple styles rather than one. We compare these two approaches, in addition to responses from GPT-4, with respect to perplexity, achieved style goals, and performance on adversarial examples (i.e. prompts with adversarial styles).

Specifically, our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A systematic study of reward formulations for RL-based control of multi-style generations
    \item A publicly available implementation for plug-and-play models with multiple style attributes
    \item An examination of the side-effects of RL-based style control, outlining scenarios in which factuality suffers and un-controlled styles shift.
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion}
We propose a straightforward approach to creating generative models with multi-style control using reinforcement learnings with a reward derived from a dynamic linear combination of discriminator outputs. This technique results in generations that largely conform to the target styles, although it is not successful for contradictory and difficult style combinations.

How styles relate to one another is an interesting an open question. If two styles rarely co-occur, is it because their combination is impossible, or is it simply rare? Does this distinction affect a language model's ability to combine the two styles? Our observations of shifts in unrelated styles hint at the complexity of this issue. Future work should consider formalizing the feasibility of different style combinations and using this as a metric of successful style combination.
