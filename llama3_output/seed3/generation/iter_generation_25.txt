\paragraph{Qualitative analysis} We examine 110 human-machine text pairs that have differing formality labels to see what qualities the texts have that may lead to this difference in formality. (We focus on the formality attribute since it is the most consistently different between human-machine text pairs.) Example text pairs can be found in Table~\ref{table:formality_qual}.

In our qualitative study, we see that $56\%$ of the machine texts include complex sentences and more independent clauses than their human counterparts. $52\%$ of the machine texts include full punctuation (e.g. completing sentences with periods, inserting commas between clauses) when the human texts do not. These factors likely contribute to the differing formality scores, but there are differences that extend past this surface level: $21\%$ of the machine texts have no discourse markers although the human text does, and machine texts also frequently use more intensifiers, adjectives, and emojis than their human counterparts. $27\%$ of the machine text use more figurative language as well.

\subsection {Second Order Effects}
\paragraph{Hypothesis}  We examine whether first-order differences in high-level textual attributes negatively affects performance for classification models trained on artificial data.
\paragraph{Experiment Setup} We train RoBERTa classifiers on synthetic and human datasets for Social Dimensions, continuing our focus on artificial data for social interactions. 

\paragraph{Metrics} We calculate the weighted F1 score on the human test set for both classifiers.

\paragraph{Result} The synthetically trained model does have decreased performance, replicating findings from \cite{moller2023prompt}; interestingly, this difference is not uniform across all social dimension labels. We run a correlation of per-label F1 scores and per-label attribute flips to see whether some attributes may be driving the performance. The correlations approach significance for sadness ($r = 0.701, p = 0.052$) and joy ($r = 0.704, p = 0.051$) and are significant for metaphor ($r = 0.807, p = 0.0156$).
