\paragraph{Result} \label{sec:figurative-results}

Our findings (see Fig~\ref{fig:high_level_features_radar}) highlight notable differences between human and LLM-generated texts. We primarily observe that texts from LLMs tend to be formal and employ more metaphoric language. \anonymous{qualitative to appendix?}

Furthermore, we noted divergences between human and LLM texts in terms of specific social-dynamics-related attributes. For instance, human texts exhibit strong anger in situations involving a social power dynamic and use irony during a more light-hearted social setting. In contrast, LLM texts tend to introduce more joy into positive Tweets, more irony in negative ones, and add neutrality into texts related to social conflicts.  Our results indicate that the resulting texts often show a different spread of high-level attributes compared to the human-authored counterparts, even though models can generate texts that align with the given social dimension labels. 

For discourse analysis, we present the distributional plots for the \textsc{HC3} dataset. We note that the human-authored documents in the dataset were originally from corresponding Question-Answering datasets, except those in the Wikipedia CS AI domain, which were taken from actual Wikipedia pages. The five figures show distinct differences in motif distributions depending on their domain. Figure \ref{fig:free_text/all_motifs} shows the corresponding network motifs. We can see that, in general, the more formal the domain (e.g., Wikipedia CS AI domain), the texts show more motifs that converge to one vertex point with the ``/'' edge label which denotes a hyperedge relation that links a lower representation to a higher one (i.e., phrases to sentences, sentences to paragraphs, etc.). We see less of those in texts from the casual domains. This suggests that domain-specific distinctiveness can arise from human-authored texts while machine-generated texts seem relatively general.

\subsection {Second Order Analysis}\label{sec:free-second}

\paragraph{Hypothesis}  We examine whether first-order differences in high-level textual attributes negatively affects performance for classification models trained on artificial data.

\paragraph{Experiment Setup} We train RoBERTa classifiers on synthetic and human datasets for Social Dimensions, continuing our focus on artificial data for social interactions. 

\paragraph{Metrics} We calculate the weighted F1 score on the human test set for both classifiers.

\paragraph{Result} The synthetically trained model does have decreased performance, replicating findings from \cite{moller2023prompt}; interestingly, this difference is not uniform across all social dimension labels. We run a correlation of per-label F1 scores and per-label attribute flips to see whether some attributes may be driving the performance. The correlations approach significance for sadness ($r = 0.701, p = 0.052$) and joy ($r = 0.704, p = 0.051$) and are significant for metaphor ($r = 0.807, p = 0.0156$).
