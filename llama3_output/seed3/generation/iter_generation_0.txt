With the advent of recent LLMs, the need as well as the challenges in distinguishing machine-generated texts has risen significantly. Various existing methodologies encompass finetuning LMs with supervised datasets, potentially utilizing techniques like contrastive learning, adversarial learning, or human-assisted learning. Additionally, there are zero-shot methods that leverage statistical features of texts or intermediate values within LLMs, watermarking techniques that introduce ``green tokens'' or embed ``secret keys'' as vectors to predicted outputs, and prompt-based approaches that make use of (another) LLM as detectors. A comprehensive overview of these approaches can be found in \citet{wu2023survey}.

While the relevant recent studies on the detection task primarily adopt surface-level features, such as distributions of n-grams and part-of-speech (POS) tags, we approach the problem from another angle, namely using hierarchical discourse features within texts. This direction is reminiscent of the work by \citet{corston-oliver-etal-2001-machine}, who, in the pre-LLM era, explored a comparable path. Their study delved into the utility of branching properties of POS trees to ascertain whether texts were machine-generated, specifically by a machine translation model. In our study, we consider discourse-level features that span over multiple sentences and paragraphs (as opposed to sentence-level POS features).

Current challenges in the field include (1) out-of-distribution (OOD) challenges where the learned detectors struggle on OOD data and LLMs, (2) potential attacks such as paraphrasing attacks where machine-generated texts are further paraphrased to change the distribution of lexical and syntactic features. This renders detectors based on surface-level features and watermarking technology ineffective. (3) Last but not least, the inherent ambiguities in the texts between human- and machine-generated ones are increasing.
