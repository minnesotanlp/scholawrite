The advancement of large language models (LLMs) has led to their widespread adoption for complex data generation tasks once exclusive to humans, such as writing new data, labeling existing data, and evaluating data. This proliferation, especially with models like ChatGPT, calls for detailed research to understand the capabilities and limitations of LLMs in various economically and efficiently executed data-intensive tasks. LLM-generated artificial data can match human data in performance across various benchmarks, yet the full implications and impact of its use in subsequent tasks remain elusive.

% risks of LLM-generated data ; no one is talking about the risks (1st order effect)
Limited research has highlighted the disparity between LLM-generated labels and human annotations \cite{reiss2023testing}, prompting calls for cross-domain evaluation against human data. There is no consensus on how artificial data differs from human data because it is a fairly new and highly fluent source of data \cite{sadasivan2023can}. 

% risk of artificial(artificial))intelligence
Recent research has also \cite{shumailov2023curse, briesch2023large} cast light on the quality and diversity issues of artificial data used for and then generated from training loops. The growing dependence on such data for its convenience and lower costs and speculations of GPT-5 training on its own generated data \footnote{Link to tweet: https://shorturl.at/kmAY2} raise concerns about an emerging cycle of AI-reliant solely on artificial inputs.

To forward the understanding of artificial data and its effects, the research questions we investigate are - 
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{RQ1} What is the nature of SOTA artificial data? How does it compare to human data, and what artifacts does it contain?
\item \textbf{RQ2} Does training on artificial data compromise performance outcomes compared to similar human-generated data? 
\item \textbf{RQ3} How specific are the artifacts of artificial data to certain types of data produced by large language models (LLMs), and how much do they apply to all types of data generated by LLMs?

\end{itemize}

% our different kinds of data
This work contributes to this understanding by taking a broad inventory of artificial data, possible ``artifacts'' or divergences from the corresponding human data, and the effects of using this data. We prioritize breadth, covering five different data types, rather than depth, to understand overarching themes in synthetic data. The five types of artificial data we examine are :
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Free Text} written by an LLM, often used to compensate for data scarcity issues.
\item \textbf{Task Labels} for classification tasks over textual input, bypassing the need for human annotators.
\item \textbf{Instruction prompts} written by LLMs for instruction fine-tuning, eliminating the need for humans to write comprehensive sets of diverse instructions.
\item \textbf{Preference}, specifically evaluating which text is better, is useful for human alignment tasks such as training reward models used in RLHF.
\item \textbf{Simulation}, where two large language model (LLM) agents converse, is a relatively unexplored approach, yet initial studies indicate its potential for enhancing model performance and simulating intricate social interactions.
\end{itemize}
% why we use this kind of classification 
We categorize LLM-generated text data based on the level of output constraint, ranging from tightly constrained (such as task labels data, which consists of discrete labels for a task) to lightly constrained (like free text data, where output is freely generated from an input). Examples of each data type are further illustrated in Figure ~\ref{fig:figure1}. This spectrum captures the wide-ranging capabilities and applications of large language models, each representing a specific type of output and process integral to the functioning of LLMs. This classification helps understand how each data type contributes to the broader AI and machine learning development landscape.

%This study extensively catalogs artificial data, identifying potential discrepancies ('artifacts') from equivalent human data and examining the impacts of using such data. 
To address RQ1, we examine \textit{first-order effects} for each data type. We define first-order effects as about the data itself, i.e. any differences or inconsistencies between the artificial data and the corresponding human data. To address RQ2, we examine \textit{second-order effects}, which we define as any differences or inconsistencies in the outcomes of NLP pipelines containing artificial data and the outcomes from corresponding pipelines containing the corresponding human data. We address RQ3 through an overview of the results of all first- and second-order experiments.

%explain iceberg
We believe that the overarching claims that LLM-generated data is comparable to human data are only scratching the surface, as these claims are mainly applicable to less complex tasks. We suggest that with tasks dealing with non-surface level problems, illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:iceberg}, LLM-generated content likely fails to capture the nuances in human data, particularly minority views with subjective datasets \cite{li2023synthetic}, intricate preferences, or specialized instructions. Moreover, high-level human attributes such as humor and sarcasm are often absent in LLM-generated texts, and the structural nuances expected in human writing are absent in LLM-written text.
