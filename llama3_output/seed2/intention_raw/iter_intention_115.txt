We identify consistent biases in LLM-generated data and outline strategies for mitigation. Representational bias in LLMs manifests as a preference for majority opinions, often at the expense of minority views. additionally, LLMs exhibit primary and recency biases, focusing on certain aspects of data and affecting task specificity and performance. This is evident in synthetic instruction datasets, where LLMs favor open-ended questions over structured tasks, raising concerns about their ability to generalize across different query types. LLMs also differ from human patterns in processing emotional content and semantic relationships. 

We find that artificial data has strong weaknesses in situations in which general, common, or well-accepted outputs are not possible. In labeling tasks, LLMs do not represent minority views well and show strong preferences for majority opinions. When creating instructions, LLMs strongly favor writing open-ended questions that are easier to answer correctly, rather than writing closed questions in which only a single answer could be correct.

Mitigation strategies : 

To mitigate these issues, we suggest enhancing data quality and diversity by incorporating domain-specific information and examples into LLM training, in order to expose LLMs to a broader range of perspectives. We propose refining model interaction and responsiveness to align LLM responses more closely with humans, especially in social and informal contexts. This involves calibrating LLMs to better understand and reflect varied emotional expressions and contextual appropriateness, aiming to reduce discrepancies in complex societal language constructs.
