pieces of evidence that LLM agents tend to flip their roles when the conversation becomes longer or one agent interrupts the conversation, etc. 
\end{itemize}
\item \textbf{Role-flipping from Different Conversation Length and Agents' Interruption Messages}:
We first found the correlation between the conversation length and the occurrence of role-flipping. We observed that role-flipping rarely happens in the first six messages (see Figure \ref{fig:camel_length}). 
This suggests that role-flipping is unlikely to happen during the initial phases of the conversation.
Next, we examine if agent interruptions are the cause of role-flipping. We found that in 88.8\% of the cases where the role-flipping occurs, the AI-User agent interrupted the conversation with a message (see Figure \ref{fig:camel_interruption}). 
These interruptions are also the most common type of interruption found in the simulated conversations. 
This result means that the role-flipping might be attributed to the agent's difficulty in following the conversation, as it happens a lot in the cases with the longest conversations.

\end{itemize}
\textbf{Conversation Length and Interruption Messages}:

Our analysis of the CAMEL AI-World dataset identified "interruption messages" in simulated conversations, which are messages that do not begin with a response to the previous message. These interruptions occur in 26.12\% of the conversations and are often related to the agent's confusion or lack of knowledge about how to respond. We also observed that in 88.8\% of cases with interruption messages, the first interruption message comes before the first role-flipping message, which suggests that the interruption messages might trigger the role-flipping behavior. 
Specifically, in 2759 conversations out of 5544 (49.7\%), the first interruption message is preceded by the first role-flipping message. 
In 736 conversations out of 5544 (13.1\%), the first interruption message is preceded by the first role-flipping message, and in 623 conversations (75.2\%), the last message in the interruption messages is also preceded by the first role-flipping message. 
This suggests that the interruption messages might trigger the role-flipping behavior in subsequent parts of the conversation.
