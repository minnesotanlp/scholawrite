\subsubsection{Locality Biases to Sentiment Lexicons} 
For sentiment preference in \textsc{P2C}, we compute a heuristic \textit{lexicon-based preference}. Using lexical-level sentiment annotations from \citet{hayati2021does}, we compute a lexical score (i.e., important words related to the prediction of text sentiment) for each sentence pair in the dataset. Based on this score, we identified the most significant lexicon within each sentence and determined which sentence in a pair more strongly conveyed the intended gold-label sentiment.

Our results show that while GPT-3 preferences are positively correlated with the simple lexical preference, human preferences are not (see Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:pref:p2c-lexicon-heatmap}). This suggests that GPT-3 takes a more surface-level approach to sentiment preference than humans do. 

We also examine how the correlations between preferences and the computed lexical-based preferences vary with different magnitudes of lexical score discrepancy in the sentence pairs, as depicted in Figure~\ref{fig:pref:corr-p2c}. To do this, we divide the paired sentences into ten percentiles based on the magnitude of their lexical score discrepancy. For example, the [1-10] group represents the top 10\% of pairs of sentences, showing the greatest difference in the pair's lexical scores.
The top percentile group, with the greatest difference in the pair's lexical scores, shows lexical-based preferences are strongly positively correlated with GPT-3's preferences, which is not the case for human preferences. In contrast, the bottom percentile group, with the smallest difference in the pair's lexical scores, shows a negligible difference between GPT-3 and humans. This suggests that \textit{when lexical differences between sentences are very strong, GPT-3 preference decisions are noticeably influenced by the local information of sentences}.
