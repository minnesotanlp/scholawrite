\begin{abstract}
Style is an integral component of text that expresses a diverse set of information, including interpersonal dynamics (e.g. formality) and the author’s emotions or attitudes (e.g. disgust). Humans often employ multiple styles simultaneously. An open question is how large language models can be explicitly controlled so that they weave together target styles when generating text: for example, to produce text that is both negative and non-toxic text. Previous work investigates the controlled generation of a single style, or else the controlled generation of a style and other attributes. In this paper, we expand into controlling multiple styles simultaneously. We demonstrate an effective yet relatively lightweight reinforcement learning (RL) approach for this controlled multi-style generation that controls multiple styles more strongly than PPLM or GPT-4 baselines. Our approach utilizes calibrated outputs from discriminators, weighted by discriminator gradient magnitudes. Ablation studies indicate that this magnitude weighting outperforms a static weighting approach. Our approach is more lightweight than RLHF and requires no explicit human preference modeling.

\section{Discussion}
We propose a straightforward approach to creating generative models with multi-style control using reinforcement learnings with a reward derived from a linear combination of discriminator outputs. This technique results in generations that largely conform to the target styles, although it is not successful for contradictory and difficult style combinations.

How styles relate to one another is an interesting an open question. If two styles rarely co-occur, is it because their combination is impossible, or is it simply rare? Does this distinction affect a language model's ability to combine the two styles? Our observations of shifts in unrelated styles hint at the complexity of this issue.

Fine-tuning for RL models is more time- and resource-intensive than plug-and-play approaches. We do find that in our experiments the models converge on a reward relatively quickly (approximately after 20k examples, or ~300 batches of size 64).

\section{Limitations}
Our work relies heavily on discriminators which may be flawed, limiting the interpretability of our results.

\section{Ethics Statement}
We observe that our training approach appears to significantly alter the LLM's understanding of facts, leading to inaccurate and misleading outputs.
We also note that training for multiple styles in this way could be used maliciously, e.g. to create specific voices for counterfeit online personas.
