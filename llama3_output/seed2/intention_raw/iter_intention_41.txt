\begin{itemize}[noitemsep,topsep=5pt,leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Free-Form Text} written by an LLM, often used to compensate for data scarcity issues and used for pretraining and/or finetuning
\item \textbf{Task Labels} for classification tasks over textual input, bypassing the need for human annotators.
\item \textbf{Instruction prompts} written by LLMs for instruction fine-tuning, eliminating the need for humans to write comprehensive sets of diverse instructions.
\item \textbf{Preference}, specifically evaluating which text is better, is useful for human alignment tasks such as training reward models used in RLHF.
\item \textbf{Simulation}, where two large language model (LLM) agents converse, is a relatively unexplored approach, yet initial studies indicate its potential for enhancing model performance and simulating intricate social interactions.
\end{itemize}

We categorize LLM-generated text data based on the level of output constraint, ranging from tightly constrained (such as task labels data, which consists of discrete labels for a task) to lightly constrained (like free-form text data, where output is freely generated from an input). Examples of each data type are further illustrated in Figure ~\ref{fig:figure1}. This spectrum captures the wide-ranging capabilities and applications of large language models, each representing a specific type of output and process integral to the functioning of LLMs. This classification helps understand how each data type contributes to the broader AI and machine learning development landscape.

To address RQ1, we examine \textit{first-order effects} for each data type. We define first-order effects as about the data itself, i.e. any differences or inconsistencies between the artificial data and the corresponding human data. To address RQ2, we examine \textit{second-order effects}, which we define as any differences or inconsistencies in the outcomes of NLP pipelines containing the artificial data and the outcomes from corresponding pipelines containing the corresponding human data. We address RQ3 through an overview of the results of all first- and second-order experiments.

\subsection{Critique of Related Work}\label{sec:free-related}
The difficulty distinguishing between human-authored and language model-generated texts (like GPT-3) is significant due to their fluency and coherence, with human evaluators often unable to differentiate at better-than-chance levels \citet{clark-etal-2021-thats,li2023deepfake}. Researchers have attempted to address this by fine-tuning pre-trained language models \cite{guo2023-how-close} or analyzing linguistic features such as part-of-speech tags, sentence structure, and common mistakes \cite{dou-etal-2022-gpt}. Our study proposes that higher-level figurative features, such as irony and emotion, may vary between LLM and human-generated texts, and we examine these differences.

Current detection models focus mostly on surface-level features and their probabilities, using techniques like random perturbations \cite{mitchell2023detectgpt} and analyzing probability spaces to distinguish AI-generated text. Additionally, while some methods \cite{verma2023ghostbuster} involve structured searches over combinations of features like n-grams, the structural and hierarchical features of documents, especially in the context of machine-generated texts, remain underexplored, indicating a potential new direction for research. While existing approaches predominantly rely on data-driven methods, utilizing text classifiers trained on labeled datasets, we intend to explore  \textit{integrating linguistic features, specifically discourse structures}. Our objective is to determine whether the analysis of discourse structures can effectively uncover potential structural peculiarities or "artifacts" that may exist in either type of text and provide valuable insights into the limitations or shortcomings of machine-generated texts.

\subsection{Datasets}\label{sec:free-data}

We utilize the \textsc{HC3} \cite{guo2023close}, \textsc{Scarecrow} \cite{dou-etal-2022-gpt}, \textsc{Deepfake} \cite{li2023deepfake}, and \textsc{Workers} \cite{moller2023prompt}. 
\paragraph{Rationale} These datasets include generated texts from both humans and LLMs in a pairwise format, which enables us to compare the difference between human-written and LLM-generated texts.
