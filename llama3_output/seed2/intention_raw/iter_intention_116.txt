With the recent advancement of NLG tasks \cite{NIPS2017rlhf, ouyang2022training, rafailov2023direct} like ChatGPT, machine-generated texts have become more prevalent than ever before. Thus, free texts represent these free-form texts generated by LLMs. 

\subsection {Critique of Related Work}\label{sec:free-related}
% \anonymous{Just copied your previous related work}

The challenge in distinguishing between human-authored and LLM-generated texts is significant because of their fluency and coherence, making it difficult for human evaluators to discern \cite{clark-etal-2021-thats,li2023deepfake}. Researchers have tried to address this by fine-tuning language models \cite{guo2023-how-close} or analyzing linguistic features like sentence structure, perplexity, and common mistakes \cite{dou-etal-2022-gpt}. Our study proposes that higher-level figurative features, such as irony and emotion, might vary between LLM and human-generated texts, and we examine these differences.

Current methods for detecting AI-generated texts focus mainly on surface-level features and their probabilities, using random perturbations \cite{mitchell2023detectgpt}. Additionally, while some methods \cite{verma2023ghostbuster} involve structured searches over combinations of features like n-grams, the structural and hierarchical features of documents, especially in the context of machine-generated texts, remain underexplored. While existing approaches predominantly rely on data-driven methods with trained text classifiers, we incorporate linguistic features, specifically \textit{discourse structures}. We seek to find out if studying discourse structures can effectively uncover artifacts in either type of text, offering insights into the limits or shortcomings of machine-generated texts.

\subsection {Datasets}\label{sec:free-data}

We utilize the \textsc{HC3} \cite{guo2023close}, \textsc{Scarecrow} \cite{dou-etal-2022-gpt}, \textsc{Deepfake} \cite{li2023deepfake}, and \textsc{Workers} \cite{moller2023prompt}. 
\paragraph{Rationale} These datasets include generated texts from both humans and LLMs in a pairwise format, which enables us to compare the difference between human-written and LLM-generated texts. 


% despite its small size as \textsc{HC3} contains questions and corresponding answers from both humans and LLMs. We filtered the data samples by a length ratio of 0.7 so that the remaining 5,529 paired documents from the five domains (finance, medicine, open QA, Reddit ELI5, and Wiki CS AI) are of similar length to each other. The \textsc{Scarecrow} dataset \citep{dou-etal-2022-gpt} that consists of 13K annotations of 1.3K human-authored and machine-generated paragraphs of English language news is also used in full. The \textsc{Deepfake} dataset \citep{li2023deepfake} covers a diverse range of paired human-machine text. This dataset sources machine text from several LLMs with different prompting strategies, some of which include a paired human text in the prompt. The \textsc{Workers} dataset \cite{moller2023prompt} has a prompting strategy that includes the full human text in the prompt as an example. For all datasets, to ensure data quality, we applied filtering criteria to remove unmatched samples and for discourse analysis, we removed samples with significant variations in text length.

\subsection {First Order Analysis}\label{sec:free-first}

\paragraph{Hypothesis} We explore how LLM-generated and human-written texts differ using (1) high-level textual attributes (e.g., \textit{irony} and \textit{metaphor}), and (2) social attributes (e.g., \textit{formality}). First, we expect that LLM-generated texts may not align with human usage of high-level textual attributes. Additionally, in complex social dynamics such as \textit{power} and \textit{conflict}, we expect that human-written texts will display more emotional sentiment. 

In addition, we examine if human authors and general-purpose language models like ChatGPT may exhibit different discourse structures in their writing, such as in casual Reddit replies versus more formal Wikipedia articles, by using graph analysis techniques.

\paragraph{Experiment Setup} Our analysis concentrates on texts from the \textsc{Deepfake}, \textsc{Workers}, and \textsc{HC3} datasets. Also, we deploy a suite of text classifiers to assess characteristics in our paired texts, such as formality \cite{rao2018dear}, emotion \cite{hartmann2022emotionenglish}, and irony \cite{barbieri2020tweeteval}. These classifiers, chiefly trained on social media datasets, evaluate key textual interactions. For metaphor analysis, we employ a token-level classifier trained on the VUA Metaphor Corpus and the TOEFL corpus \cite{li2023framebert} that sourced from non-social media datasets and did not primarily focus on interpersonal aspects. We then incorporated a qualitative examination of 100 machine-generated and human-authored Tweets to investigate the linguistic discrepancy between human-written and LLM-generated texts. 

For the discourse analysis, we first apply an existing discourse parser \citep{liu-etal-2021-dmrst} to the paired texts. For a given document, the parser produces a hierarchical discourse tree that adheres to a discourse framework called the Rhetorical Structure Theory \citep{mann1987rhetorical}. We note that this hierarchical tree can be seen as a recursive hypergraph in graph theory. By converting the trees to hypergraphs, we can conduct some standard graph analyses such as computing the distribution of network motifs of varying sizes. These network motifs are small directed subgraphs with edges labeled with a discourse relation and can be considered as a building block or a ``vocabulary'' of larger graphs. We check the differences in these motif distributions and validate if they represent any meaningful patterns in discourse structures, and thus, in the ways the documents are written or generated. 

\paragraph{Metrics} Out of the paired human-LLM texts, we count how often the label \textsc{flips}, i.e. differs between the original human and the resulting machine text, for each category of high-level attributes. Since high-level textual attributes are highly domain-dependent (e.g., formality is often present in news or scientific articles but rarely present in tweets), we focus on examining the flips for each domain of our datasets. 

We examine each document graph $G$ in a domain by counting the occurrence of a particular motif or subgraph $M$ in $G$, and then normalize these counts based on the size of the graph (i.e., the number of edges $|E|$). This is aggregated over all instances and averaged across domains.  From there, we subtract the averaged distribution of human-authored texts from that of machine-generated texts, thus showing a discourse pattern more common in one type of text than the other.

Our findings (see Figure~\ref{fig:high_level_features_radar}) highlight notable differences between human and LLM-generated texts. We primarily observe that texts from LLMs tend to be formal and employ more metaphoric language. More details are presented in Table \label{table:formality_qual} in Appendix \ref{sec:appendix:free}.

Furthermore, we noted divergences between human and LLM texts in terms of specific social-dynamics-related attributes. For instance, human texts exhibit strong anger in situations involving a social power dynamic and use irony during a more light-hearted social setting. In contrast, LLM texts tend to introduce more joy into positive Tweets, more irony in negative ones, and add neutrality into texts related to social conflicts.  Our results indicate that the resulting texts often show a different spread of high-level attributes compared to the human-authored counterparts, even though models can generate texts that align with the given social dimension labels. 

For discourse analysis, we present the distributional plots for the \textsc{Deepfake} dataset focusing on the three domains of texts with increasing formality in style: ``yelp,'' ``cmv,'' and ``xsum.'' Figures \ref{fig:free_text/dist_diff_reddit}- \ref{fig:free_text/dist_diff_openqa} in Appendix \ref{sec:appendix:free} show distinct differences in motif distributions depending on their domain. In general, the more formal the domain, the texts show more motifs that converge to one vertex point with the ``/'' edge label which denotes a hyperedge relation that links a lower representation to a higher one (i.e., phrases to sentences, sentences to paragraphs, etc.). We see less of those in texts from the casual domains. This suggests that domain-specific distinctiveness can arise from human-authored texts while machine-generated texts seem relatively general.

\subsection {Second Order Analysis}\label{sec:free-second}

\paragraph{Hypothesis}  We examine whether first-order differences in high-level textual attributes negatively affects performance for classification models trained on artificial data.

\paragraph{Experiment Setup} We train RoBERTa classifiers on synthetic and human datasets for Social Dimensions, continuing our focus on artificial data for social interactions. 

\paragraph{Metrics} We calculate the weighted F1 score on the human test set for both classifiers.

\paragraph{Result} The synthetically trained model does have decreased performance, replicating findings from \cite{moller2023prompt}; interestingly, this difference is not uniform across all social dimension labels. We run a correlation of per-label F1 scores and per-label attribute flips to see whether some attributes may be driving the performance. The correlations approach significance for sadness ($r = 0.701, p = 0.052$) and joy ($r = 0.704, p = 0.051$) and are significant for metaphor ($r = 0.807, p = 0.0156$).
