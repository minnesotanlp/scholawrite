\subsection{First Order Analysis}
\paragraph{Hypothesis}
Our investigation focuses on how LLM-annotated datasets represent minority opinions, especially in subjective contexts, hypothesizing a bias towards majority viewpoints in LLMs that could lead to homogenized responses and diminished diversity in datasets requiring nuanced judgment and complex decision-making.
\paragraph{Experiment Setup}
We utilized various open-sourced and commercially available, instruction-tuned LLMs like Vicuna \cite{vicuna2023}, Koala \cite{koala_blogpost_2023}, Baize \cite{xu2023baize}, GPT-3.5-Turbo \cite{brown2020language}, and LLaMA2 \cite{touvron2023llama} as annotators to generate labels for dataset tasks. 
In our 'intra-model' setup, we grouped LLM annotators by their architecture and training methods, altering their response order to simulate a diverse group of human annotators. Conversely, the 'inter-model' setup grouped models by hyperparameters, yielding labels from different models, acknowledging that some models, like GPT-3.5 Turbo, have greater capacities akin to varying proficiencies among human annotators.
\paragraph{Metrics}
\noindent \textsc{Match Score}: The match score measures the agreement between human and model annotations, calculated as the number of matches divided by the total number of samples.

\noindent \textsc{Wasserstein Distance}: This metric evaluates the similarity between two probability distributions, particularly with ordinal values, and is conceptually akin to the minimum effort required to reshape one distribution into the other, hence its alternative name, Earth Mover’s Distance.
