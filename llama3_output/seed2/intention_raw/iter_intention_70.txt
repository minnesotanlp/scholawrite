\subsection{Mitigating Biases from LLM Generated Task Labels}\label{sec:task-mitigation}

Majority voting and averaging, standard methods for deriving single ground truth labels from multiple annotations, often fail to capture the systematic disagreements among annotators, especially in subjective tasks like detecting affect, aggression, and hate speech. Through our experiments, we find that LLM models tend to under-represent minority opinions, and this risks marginalizing minority perspectives, potentially reinforcing societal disparities \cite{prabhakaran2021releasing}. Monte Carlo Estimation (MCE) and Log Probability Estimation (LPE) are other ways to find the distributional difference between human and LLM task labels that have also been recently explored \cite{lee2023large}.  Thus, there is a need to preserve disagreement in LLM annotations so that they more accurately mirror human annotations. This could be also be done using Bayesian methods \cite{paun2018comparing}, integrating disagreements into loss functions \cite{plank2014learning}, or modeling several annotators’ labels as multiple tasks \cite{davani2022dealing}. 

There is also a rising need for validation using human datasets \cite{pangakis2023automated}. Several studies testing LLMs on widely used datasets potentially suffer from data contamination, where LLMs may memorize information, leading to misleadingly strong performance that may not extend to new datasets and tasks, highlighting the need for clear guidelines to ensure effective and accurate use of these tools.
