IRequest for a more in-depth study of the artifacts in LLM-generated data, particularly in the context of graph-structured data, which is important for social science research. (1) LLMs have demonstrated some success in handling graph-structured data, which have been used to build complex relationships and patterns. (2) However, little work has been done to understand the nature of LLM-generated graph data. 
Our opinion is that understanding the nature of LLM-generated graph data is urgent, given both the recent outpouring of its usage and the likelihood that this trend continues to grow. Without a robust understanding of LLM-generated graph data, it is difficult to thoroughly interpret results that rely on its usage, which could hinder progress in the field.

\textbf{Our main contributions}, rather than merely reporting our experimental results, are:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
    \item We propose a novel approach to creating LLM-generated graph datasets, which we term "graph zooming." We propose that instead of generating the entire graph, we generate a subgraph that is larger than a single node but smaller than the entire graph. This method is a good way to build a diverse and complex graph dataset. 
    \item We also create a decision boundary between the "presence" of the edge information and the "notch" effect, which seems to be an important factor for the graph structure. 
    \item We also investigate the impact of graph structure on LLM prompting, and the effective node classification tasks on the text and image modalities.
\end{itemize}
