\subsection{Multi-Reward Control Formulation Evaluation}\label{sec:reward_eval}
We train several versions of models with Informal-Negative and Formal-Negative target styles to investigate which of the reward formulations outlined in Sec~\ref{sec:reward_formulations} is most effective. For a summary of our study on Informal-Negative see Table~\ref{tab:ablation}; full results are in Appendix Table~\ref{tab:ablation-large}. We find that for softmax rewards, the models make only small improvements on controlling styles. We hypothesize that a contributing factor may be poor confidence calibration of our discriminators' softmax scores. To correct this, we implement a calibrated softmax reward using the confidence calibration technique proposed in \citet{guo2017calibration}. This results in the Expected Calibration Error (ECE) for sentiment and formality discriminators decreases from $0.133$ to $0.016$ and $0.267$ to $0.111$ respectively. These calibrated softmax rewards do offer some improvements relative to the un-calibrated softmax rewards, but improvements are modest.

Reward formulations using raw logits, on the other hand, tend to control the styles better at the cost of fluency, as measured by the average perplexity of the generations. We also anecdotally observed that the logit-reward models often developed \textbf{highly repetitive reward hacking strategies} when the KL coeffecient was not sufficiently high; see examples in Table~\ref{tab:reward_hacks}.


Both the binary reward approach and dynamic gradient weighting (GradWt) approach produces sizable improvements over the softmax or logit based rewards while maintaining generation quality. The GradWt approach outperforms the binary approach on all counts for the Negative-Informal combination ($60.25\%$ of GradWt generations contain both styles versus $56.80$ for binary). Generations from the binary approach are also more repetitive than those from GradWt. In the Negative-Formal combination, results are mixed: $38.90\%$ of the generations in the binary approach model contain both styles versus $37.50\%$ in the GradWt approach, with binary having substantially more target fomality and GradWt having substantially more target sentiment.
Based on results from these experiments, we chose to implement the GradWt approach for our remaining two- and three-style 

We note that particularly when combining multiple non-orthogonal styles, a simple linear combination of scores can make learning difficult for the model: the model may be able to easily increase its reward by maximizing results from only one discriminator and get ``stuck'' without learning how to obtain good results from all discriminators. We conjecture that both the binarizing and dynamic weighting approaches alleviate this issue.
