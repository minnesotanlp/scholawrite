\subsection{Second-order Analysis}\label{sec:task-second}
\paragraph{Hypothesis}
We train an LLM on both human and machine-generated annotations to identify potential second-order effects and biases. The following section investigates the hypothesis that biases become more pronounced over time with a concentrated distribution highlighting specific stereotypes and assumptions.
% Additionally, we expect more entropy over time among the annotations generated by inter or intra-model LLMs because of how the model learns. 
% We know that models are eager learners in that their weights will change easily during training or fine-tuning \anonymous{does this need citing?}. Humans, on the other hand, are governed by societal or self-rules that make us more resistant to change. Therefore, we expect a model trained on LLM-generated annotations to change its annotation decisions more easily (label-flipping) compared to a model trained on human-generated annotations. The more stable nature of human annotations can help us retain more diversity over time.
% \paragraph{Experiment Setup}
We fine-tune a multi-headed RoBERTa-base classifier \cite{liu2019roberta} on both the human and machine-generated annotations. The artificial data was generated by models mentioned in Section \ref{sec:task-first}. Before fine-tuning, we order the training data annotations in four ways. We create two versions based on frequency (the more popular opinions are placed first). We do this on both a dataset-wide scale and individual-level scale \cite{jung2023clusterguided}. We use both because performance differences will depend on the data - in other words, whether knowing the majority for other instances in the dataset will help the annotation process. The ordering process improves the inference capabilities of the multi-head architecture since it helps narrow down likely annotations. Another version is based on numerical sorting and the last is shuffled. To remove inequality in the number of training samples, we randomly selected a maximum of 5000 instances and fine-tuned a RoBERTa-base model on those subsets.  

\paragraph{Metrics}
We focus on distributional differences since we are interested specifically on the efinct of artificial data on dataset diversity. We use the same metrics as our first-order experiments.

Our results section shows that LLMs tend to under-represent minority opinions. This risks marginalizing minority perspectives, potentially reinforcing societal disparities \cite{prabhakaran2021releasing}. For subjective tasks, we find that LLMs  over-estimate the majority label distribution. This could result in  which may not reflect the true distribution of human annotations.
