\item \textsc{Wasserstein distance}: This metric compares probability distributions, especially with ordinal values. It is also called Earth Mover’s distance because it can be interpreted informally as the minimum energy cost of moving and transforming a pile of dirt from one probability distribution to the shape of the other distribution.
    
    \item \textsc{Kullback–Leibler (KL) Divergence}: This metric is a measure of the difference between two probability distributions, expected. It indicates the extent of how likely it is to generate data from one distribution given the existence of the other distribution. 
    \item \textsc{Cosine similarity}: This metric is used to measure the similarity between two non-normalized vectors. It is also called the dot product because it is the dot product of two vectors with themselves. 
    \item \textsc{Majority voting}: This is a technique used to generate labels for classification problems with a majority voting strategy, which means that if the majority of the labels are 0, the final label is also predicted to be 0; if the majority of the labels are 2, the final label is also predicted to be 2. 
    \item \textsc{Minority voting}: This is the opposite of majority voting. We use minority voting to estimate the second label of a generation. If the majority of the labels are 0, we know that the non-majority label is also 2. \\
    \item \textsc{Softmax}: This is a technique used to find the softmax scores of a generation. We take the softmax scores of all of the target classes, add them together, and make sure their sum is 1 (or some other value that is not 0). The final label is the class with the highest softmax score. \\
    \item \textsc{Calibrated:} Confidence calibration is a technique to make the model more confident in its predictions and less likely to produce incorrect predictions. It is also used to make the softmax scores seem more like a probability. The calibration technique works by creating a relationship between the softmax scores and the probability of the predicted outcome. For example, if the softmax score for the predicted outcome is high, it implies that the outcome is likely, so the probability should be high as well (around 0.9). On the other hand, if the softmax score is very low, the outcome is unlikely, so the probability should be low as well (around 0.1). \\
    \item \textsc{Adversarial Eval}: This is a technique used to generate adversarial examples. We use this technique to generate examples with adversarial styles. The adversarial examples are generated by adding the adversarial  to the target style. For example, if the target style is positive, the adversarial example should be negative. \\

    \item \textbf{Annotator 2-Style Models}: We train two 2-style models, one with a bigram-based preference and the other with a lexical-based preference. We then test each model on 1000 samples from the other datasets and measure the matching accuracy on that test set. \\

    \item \textbf{Simulation}: We use a language model to generate text in a social context. The conversation between two agents can be considered as a simulation of human conversation. We evaluate the generated conversation by measuring the role-flipping ratio (i.e., the ratio of the number of role-flipped messages to the total number of messages). 
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Metrics}
\label{sec:exp_results}
For all the first-order experiments, we report the following metrics:
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{Match Score}: The match score measures the agreement between the human annotations and the model's predictions. It is calculated as the number of matches divided by the total number of samples.
    \item \textbf{Variation}: In ordinal data, where differences between levels aren't uniform as in interval or ratio data, statistical measures like standard deviation or variance are still useful for gauging the spread of opinions, indicating the extent of agreement or disagreement among annotations rather than providing exact distance measurements.
    \item \textbf{Wasserstein Distance}: This metric evaluates the similarity between two probability distributions, particularly with ordinal values, and is conceptually akin to the minimum effort required to reshape one distribution into the other, hence its alternative name, Earth Mover’s Distance.
\end{enumerate}
