_with respect to the target styles, and second, whether the generations maintain linguistic quality (i.e., a model with a \textit{positive} control could simply complete every prompt with ``This is great, I love it!''). We find that the first issue is not unique to sentiment control, as it also appears in other control settings such as formality and irony. We hypothesize that this may be due to poor confidence calibration for discriminators, particularly in out-of-domain texts. Finally, we note that particularly when combining multiple styles, a simple linear combination of scores can make learning difficult for the model. Intuitively, in the two-style case, the model may reach a plateau in which the score from one discriminator is very high and the score from the other is very low. We attempt to guide the model towards making improvement with respect to both rewards in two ways: one is the binary reward formulation approach, which simply assigns a score of $-1$ for each style the generation does not fulfill and a score of $1$ for each style the generation does fulfill. This approach, inspired by binary RL formulations in sparse environments such as ours, means that the value model must be able to understand how many styles a generation will fulfill, rather than understand the individual score of each style.
