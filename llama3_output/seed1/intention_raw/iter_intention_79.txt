These artificial data can replace human data without performance degradation on many benchmarks, leading to increased efficiency and cost savings. 
However, there is a notable misalignment between human rationales and large language model (LLM) judgments in question-answering tasks, attributed to biases in LLMs when evaluating text quality. 
For example, studies have identified various biases in LLMs like GPT-4, including position, length, and self-enhancement biases \cite{zheng2023judging, wang2023large}. 
Additionally, research on 15 different LLMs of varying size revealed a range of cognitive biases \cite{koo2023benchmarking} affecting their preferences in pairwise evaluations, with a common order bias where LLMs tend to prefer the first-ordered response. 
This work also shows that larger models, such as GPT-4 and ChatGPT, often show an egocentric bias, favoring their responses, and that smaller models are also susceptible to multiple cognitive biases. 

Therefore, it is important to first understand the nature of human data and the potential biases or errors present in artificial data, and second, to study the effects of using this kind of data in downstream tasks.
