Given the increased use of AI-assisted scholarly writing and literature review tools, it is important to study  this interaction between human users and AI-assisted interfaces. We must consider the intrinsic values and objectives of academic writing. The act of writing is a dynamic aspect of academic inquiry, not a procedural task to complete at the end of the research process. The writing process works in a feedback loop, allowing the writer to refine, solidify, or even challenge their understanding; it informs the writer almost as much as it does the reader. Through the practice of writing, researchers not only contribute to the body of scientific knowledge, but also develop their own cognitive and analytical skills. In this context, the use of Large Language Models (LLMs) like GPT-4 to draft paper sections should be considered carefully. AI-assisted tools can reduce some of the more mundane aspects of writing, empowering the researcher to focus on creative and interpretive tasks. However, there is also a risk of over-reliance which may serve to disconnect the writer from engaging immersively with their work. 

Consequently, it is important to explore the challenges of evaluating AI-assisted written texts. Our research began initially with an investigation into GPT-4's ability to generate Related Work sections without human collaboration, and we intended to evaluate its outputs by comparing them with the original, human-written Related Work section. This became an impossible task due to the open-ended nature of scholarly composition. For any given paper, there may be dozens of ways to compose a related work section that best frames the work-in-progress. It is insufficient to sort citances into True-Positive/False-Positive/False-Negative categories, when the decision to use a citation is dependent on the larger context of the paper; only looking at the similarity of the sections using ROUGE scores falls short for similar reasons.
