\begin{itemize}[noitemsep,topsep=5pt,leftmargin=*]
\item We find several cases where artificial data diverges from human data. The divergence in LLM performance is frequently attributed to their limited, superficial comprehension of tasks, as evidenced by their consistent overrepresentation of majority opinions across all datasets, including those with highly subjective content. Additionally, LLMs struggle to align with minority viewpoints and exhibit less variability in annotations for subjective tasks than humans, indicating a lack of understanding about the controversial nature of certain labeling tasks and a tendency to mirror only majority responses.

\subsection{Suggestions for each data type}
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep,topsep=5pt,leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Free-Form Texts}: Mitigate the ``curse of generality'' in language models by incorporating domain-specific information during text generation, using few-shot prompting with relevant examples. Enhance discourse structure modeling or adjust decoding strategies to improve the human-like quality of machine-generated texts, such as re-ranking candidates based on discourse patterns. Moreover, in the evaluation of machine-generated texts, it could be beneficial to incorporate high-level features such as the \textit{``formality score,''} \textit{``extent of label flipping,''} and \textit{``discourse motif distribution,''} in conjunction with conventional metrics like ROUGE, BLEU, or METEOR. This approach enhances the comprehensiveness of the evaluation, enabling a more nuanced assessment of the resemblance between human-written and machine-generated texts within a given domain.
\item \textbf{Task Labels}: Majority voting, commonly used for generating task labels, tends to overlook minority views and disagreements among annotators, particularly in highly subjective tasks. Rather than evaluating LLM task labels solely on cost, speed, and accuracy of annotation, we propose alternative metrics like \textit{label distributional difference} or an analysis of the \textit{variation in individual text annotations} to highlight discrepancies between LLM and human-generated labels. It's crucial to validate LLMs with human datasets to avoid data biases and falsely inflated performance metrics, underscoring the importance of establishing clear guidelines for various tasks. 
\item \textbf{Instructions}: We found that synthetic datasets contain various types of errors and that artificially adding errors to fine-tuning data resulted in worse performance. We recommend that the reader mitigate these issues by closely \textit{inspecting data for errors}, performing automatic preprocessing, and generating diverse data. Researchers may want to consider creating datasets following observed trends in their data or using existing datasets that have been pre-analized. For example, the \textsc{Unnatural Instructions} dataset shows a trend towards generative or open-QA tasks. While there are a few openly available datasets that contain machine-generated instructions, they face the challenge of including a diverse range of tasks. 
\item \textbf{Preference}: LLMs show a stronger tendency to show locality bias or be sensitive to local linguistic cues of texts that are more salient than humans when judging their preferences. We alert researchers to carefully use LLM-generated preferences by first detecting \textit{differences between human and LLM preferences} and providing detailed \textit{guidelines in prompts to include a more nuanced understanding of sentences} rather than focusing on specific locally salient features. Improving the quality of LLM-generated preferences is crucial to reduce misalignment issues and ensure better alignment with human preferences.
