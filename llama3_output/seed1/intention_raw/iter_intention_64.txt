The writing process works in a feedback loop, allowing the writer to refine, solidify, or even challenge their understanding; it informs the writer almost as much as it does the reader. Through the practice of writing, researchers not only contribute to the body of scientific knowledge but also develop their own cognitive and analytical skills \cite{doi:10.1177/1077800408318280}. In this context, the use of Large Language Models (LLMs) like GPT-4 to draft paper sections should be considered carefully. While AI-assisted tools can reduce some of the more mundane aspects of writing, there is also a risk of over-reliance which may serve to disconnect the writer from engaging immersively with their work. 

One important aspect of studying LLM-generated or collaboratively-generated texts is to be able to evaluate them in a way that reflects academic standards of what a high-quality Related Work section does. This requires us to first define what makes a Related Work section ``high-quality'', and second, to develop a method for subjectively measuring relative quality. For our purposes, we define quality using the following description from a guide on writing literature reviews from Carnegie Mellon's Student Academic Success Center\footnote{\url{https://www.cmu.edu/student-success/other-resources/handouts/comm-supp-pdfs/literature-reviews.pdf}}: 
\begin{quote}
    A literature review selects relevant past literature and \textbf{CONNECTS}, \textbf{SYNTHESIZES}, and sometimes \textbf{EVALUATES} these texts/studies, putting the authors in conversation with each other \cite{cmu_litreview}.
\end{quote}  
In addition to the above definition, the guide states that an author should establish their work's relevance to their research field, demonstrate their understanding of the preexisting discourse and findings on the topic, and describe the research gap being filled by their new work. Central to this definition are the choices the author makes regarding which works to discuss in conjunction with each other, and how well those choices support the framing of the full context of their work. 

Determining how to measure the quality of LLM-assisted texts is a non-trivial problem. In order to evaluate texts in a way that reflects the importance of author decision-making and full-paper contexts, we set up a test bed that includes ten previously written papers that serve as ``works in progress'' for our experimental setup. Each of these samples is accompanied by the abstracts of each paper that is cited in its original Related Work section. The LLM is provided with a ``work-in-progress'' with the original Related Work section redacted, as well as all of the cited abstracts, and is then tasked with helping the user compose a Related Work section. 

We initially attempted a one-to-one comparison between the citation occurrences in the LLM-assisted text and the original human text. However, this became an impossible task due to the open-ended nature of scholarly composition. For any given paper, there may be several ways to compose a related work section that best frames the work-in-progress. It is insufficient to sort citances into True-Positive/False-Positive/False-Negative categories when the decision to use a citation is dependent on the larger context of the paper and the goals of the author. Furthermore, this analysis is difficult to automate and may involve some subjectivity. 

To address these challenges in evaluation, we developed a novel evaluation method based on the analysis of citation graphs, where nodes are cited works and the edges represent concurrences of cited works within the same sentence. By examining the structural complexity and interconnectedness in citation graphs rather than on fine-grained authorial choices, we can approximate the degree to which these texts perform literary synthesis in a way that is objective, reproducible, and scalable. This method allows us to quantitatively assess how effectively the text integrates and contextualizes multiple sources, a key component of high-quality Related Work sections. It offers a more objective lens through which to evaluate the ability of LLM-assisted compositions in achieving the essential goal of Related Work composition: to not only gather related works but also to combine diverse pieces of research into a coherent narrative.
