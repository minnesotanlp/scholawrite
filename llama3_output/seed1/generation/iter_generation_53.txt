\subsection{Main findings}
%\noindent \textbf{
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep,topsep=5pt,leftmargin=*]
\item We find several cases in which artificial data diverges from human data. The divergence often stems from LLMs having only a superficial or shallow understanding of the task. For instance, LLMs consistently overrepresent the majority opinion across all datasets we examined, even in highly subjective datasets. Further, they rarely match minority opinions and show fewer variations in responses to subjective tasks than humans. That is, LLMs do not appear to understand which labeling tasks might be controversial, and they also appear only to understand how the majority will respond to a labeling question. 
    
\item LLMs generating text for certain contexts apply textual styles unnaturally, and often simplistically, despite being given examples of real human texts: for example, when writing `positive Tweets,' LLMs overwhelmingly employ \textit{joy} when in actually, only a small fraction of the example positive Tweets in the LLM prompt exhibit joy. LLMs also do not vary discourse patterns across different domains as humans do, instead employing the same discourse patterns. We argue that this demonstrates a superficial understanding of how discourse and textual style interact with various contexts.
    
\item We find that LLMs also employ a superficial understanding of preference tasks. For instance, LLMs deciding which sentence has a stronger positive sentiment make decisions that are strongly correlated with the relative number of standalone positive tokens in each sentence -- in contrast to humans, who show only a small correlation and presumably base their preference decision on other factors.
