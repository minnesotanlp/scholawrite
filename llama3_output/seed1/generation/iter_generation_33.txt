The use of LLMs in datasets could lead to an overemphasis on superficial patterns and a lack of attention to the nuances of human data, especially minority views. Additionally, the reliance on such data for its convenience and lower costs and speculations of GPT-5 training on its own generated data \footnote{Link to tweet: https://shorturl.at/kmAY2} raise concerns about an emerging cycle of AI-reliant solely on artificial inputs.

To forward the understanding of artificial data and its effects, the research questions we investigate are - 
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{RQ1} What is the nature of SOTA artificial data? How does it compare to human data, and what artifacts does it contain?
\item \textbf{RQ2} Does training on artificial data compromise performance outcomes compared to similar human-generated data? 
\item \textbf{RQ3} How specific are the artifacts of artificial data to certain types of data produced by large language models (LLMs), and how much do they apply to all types of data generated by LLMs?

\end{itemize}
% our different kinds of data
This work contributes to this understanding by taking a broad inventory of artificial data, possible ``artifacts'' or divergences from the corresponding human data, and the effects of using this data. We prioritize breadth, covering five different data types, rather than depth, to understand overarching themes in synthetic data that transcend domain-specific scenarios.

The five types of artificial data we examine are
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Text} written by an LLM, often used to compensate for data scarcity issues.
\item \textbf{Labels} for classification tasks over textual input, bypassing the need for human annotators.
\item \textbf{Instruction prompts} written by LLMs for use in instruction fine-tuning, eliminating the need for humans to write comprehensive sets of diverse instructions.
\item \textbf{Preference}, specifically the evaluation of which text is better, is useful for human alignment tasks such as training reward models used in RLHF.
\item \textbf{Simulation}, in which two LLM agents engage in a conversation, is a relatively underexplored paradigm, but early research suggests use cases in improving model performance and modeling complex social interactions. \anonymous{It's a little odd that this is a full sentence while the others aren't. \end{itemize}

We categorize LLM-generated text data based on the level of output constraint, ranging from tightly constrained (such as task labels data, which consists of discrete labels for a task) to lightly constrained (like free-form text data, where output is freely generated from an input). Examples of each data type are further illustrated in Figure ~\ref{fig:figure1}. This spectrum captures the wide-ranging capabilities and applications of large language models, each representing a specific type of output and process integral to the functioning of LLMs. This classification helps understand how each data type contributes to the broader AI and machine learning development landscape.

%This study extensively catalogs artificial data, identifying potential discrepancies ('artifacts') from equivalent human data and examining the impacts of using such data. 
To address RQ1, we examine \textit{first-order effects} for each data type. We define first-order effects as about the data itself, i.e. any differences or inconsistencies between the artificial data and the corresponding human data. To address RQ2, we examine \textit{second-order effects}, which we define as any differences or inconsistencies in the outcomes of NLP pipelines containing artificial data and the outcomes from corresponding pipelines containing the corresponding human data. We address RQ3 through an overview of the results of all first- and second-order experiments.

We contend that the general assertion that LLM-generated data matches human data is superficial, as it mainly applies to simple tasks. As depicted in Figure \ref{fig:iceberg}, for more complex tasks, LLM-generated content probably misses the subtleties found in human data, especially in areas like minority perspectives, complex preferences, or detailed instructions. Additionally, LLM-generated texts often lack higher-level human traits such as humor and sarcasm, and they don't exhibit the structural intricacies typical of human writing.
