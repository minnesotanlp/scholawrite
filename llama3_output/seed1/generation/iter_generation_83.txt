%\textbf{Graph data challenges with LLMS, General gaps in doing reasoning tasks on graphs}
While there has been progress in interpreting multi-modal information \cite{yin2023survey}, integrating graph understanding into Large Language Models (LLMs) remains a developing area. Current challenges include LLMs' difficulty directly processing complex graph-structured data \cite{guo2023gpt4graph}, necessitating innovative input encoding and prompt design \cite{fatemi2023talk,chen2023exploring} to provide graph-structured information to LLMs. However, existing research often uses constrained setups with limited real-world graph sizes \cite{guo2023gpt4graph} or synthetic graphs \cite{wang2023can}, highlighting a research gap in how to effectively feed large real-world graphs into LLMs due to their context window limitations.

This paper investigates the impact of different modalities for encoding global and local graph structures, focusing on node classification tasks, and compares three modalities: \textit{Text}, \textit{Motif}, and \textit{Image} (See Figure \ref{fig:fig1}). Text modality offers detailed local insights but becomes verbose for large graphs \cite{bubeck2023sparks}, often exceeding the input limits of models like GPT-4. The Motif modality is suggested to address this, capturing essential patterns in a node's vicinity for a balanced local-global perspective. Additionally, Image modality is proposed, utilizing fewer tokens to convey a more global view of the node's neighborhood, a method enhanced by the vision capabilities of the newly released GPT-4V \cite{openai_gpt4}. 
Finding the optimal prompt of input formats is a notably complex challenge, with text modality encoding requiring extensive exploration compared to the simpler, more human-readable image modality, which faces less tokens and is more amenable to qualitative analysis. In our evaluations, we balance informativeness and prompt conciseness across all modalities using a combination of metrics.

The exploration and research required for effective text modality encoding in Large Language Models is vast, in contrast to the simpler implementation of image modality, which benefits from its alignment with human readability. We evaluate all modalities using a combination of metrics that balance informativeness with prompt conciseness.
